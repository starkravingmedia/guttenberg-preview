The Wall / The Refusal
Bartleby is a copyist. Or was. He works in a Manhattan office building where the meaning of work has come undone. He is thin. His eyes are very still. He has never raised his voice. He never will.

The Show

Beat 1 Montage Opening

The set is sparse. A desk. A monitor. A single camera. The backdrop is dark blue, almost black, with no visible branding. This emptiness is intentional. The space seems to absorb sound. Danny Dan-Man is in the chair, visible, effusive.

He is smiling at the camera with the trained energy of someone who has done this ten thousand times. His energy is pitched perfectly — not too manic, not too corporate. It lands in that exact frequency where it seems genuine even though nothing about this setup is genuine. His teeth are very white. His eyes are very bright.

“Good morning, story seekers,” Danny says, and the phrase itself is a formula that has been tested and works. “I’m Danny, and this is ‘What’s Your Story?’ — the show where your experience matters and your voice gets heard. Today we have four incredible submissions. But before we dive in, let me say: submitting here takes courage. Telling your truth in a space like this — it’s not nothing. You are not nothing.”

He pauses. The pause is calculated. It is meant to feel genuine but it is the opposite of genuine. It is a technique he learned from someone who learned it from someone else. It lands in the same place every time.

The camera cuts to the muggle roster: Marcus in his home office, desk too clean, plant clearly purchased for the Zoom background. Jennifer in what appears to be a bedroom, wearing a crystal necklace that catches light. David in an expensive kitchen, very still. Sarah in her apartment, the background softly blurred, her hands visible in her lap, shaking slightly.

Kevin is actually filming himself in his podcast studio setup — he has taken the effort to set this up properly and he is proud of it. Rachel is at a library desk, trying to look casual but positioned too carefully. Thomas is in what appears to be a real office, not a home office, and he is already frowning slightly. Avery is in their car, the only submission from a moving location, and they are very small in the frame, very aware they are being recorded.

“Let’s start with our first submission,” Danny continues, and his smile shifts microscopically — not less, but different. More professional. Less warm. “We have a lot of ground to cover today.”

Kade’s Assessment
Kade is not watching. Kade is listening to Danny’s word choice. When he said “your experience matters,” his eye moved to the off-camera spot. When he said “you are not nothing,” there was something false in his tone register. Kade makes note. Danny is uncomfortable. Not frightened. Uncomfortable. This is useful information.
Roll: INT check (Kade’s perception) d20 + 4 = 18. Kade identifies Danny’s pattern variance. The earpiece must be feeding him something he wouldn’t normally say.
Beat 2 Muggle Interviews and Show Exposition

The show format is simple: Danny asks each muggle to tell their story in ninety seconds. No more. The timer is visible. It creates pressure. It creates truth. People say the most honest things when they have no time to lie.

Marcus speaks first. He talks about a marketing campaign that seemed to write itself, about opening a file on his computer and finding language he didn’t remember writing, brand messaging that was somehow perfect, almost alive. He frames it as serendipity. He frames it as the universe sending a message. Danny nods. Kade, listening, makes a note: Marcus will lie to himself no matter what happens. Useless.

Jennifer is next. She talks about the tarot reading that came true, about drawing the Hermit when she was alone, about understanding that the solitude was calling her to something. She is breathless. She wants to be part of something mystical. Danny leans forward. He is actually interested in Jennifer. This is not a good sign for Jennifer’s survival probability.

David speaks in precise language about a legal document he reviewed that contradicted itself — the same passage meant two completely different things depending on which way you read it. He was not supposed to notice. He noticed. He assumes his intelligence will protect him in whatever comes next. It will not. Kade notes: High competence. Low wisdom. Likely to trust his reasoning past the point of survival.

Sarah is crying before she speaks. She says “I don’t know why I’m here. I don’t know if I should be here. I wrote something true and I don’t know what true means when you broadcast it.” Danny extends actual human kindness toward her. His hand gestures toward the camera. His smile becomes gentler. For a moment, he is not performing. This moment costs him. Kade sees it. Danny is tired. Danny is not sure anymore which parts of this are real and which are script.

Kevin pitches his podcast. He is here to create content. He says this directly. He has no shame about it. He will film everything. He will document. He will treat this as raw material. Kade’s assessment: Transparent motivation. Will survive longer because he will never believe he is in actual danger. Will die confused.

Rachel references the novels. She names them. She demonstrates that she has read the source material. She assumes knowledge is protection. She assumes understanding the map is equivalent to being able to navigate the territory. This assumption will cost her. Kade makes note: Intellectual confidence. Actual helplessness. Dangerous combination.

Thomas says almost nothing. He says “Something is wrong with this show. I don’t know what. But something is wrong.” Danny’s smile tightens. His eye goes to the off-camera spot. Kade speaks into his earpiece: “Ask him about the book.” Danny immediately shifts. His energy changes. He asks Thomas about the estate sale book, about whether he ever found the original, about whether the rewritten version was better or worse.

Thomas says: “It was more honest. The original was the book the author intended. The rewritten version knew things the author never knew. I don’t think the rewriting was an accident.”

This answer is not on the script that Danny has been following. Kade leans forward, listening.

Avery speaks last, in a voice that is very quiet. They describe the dissociation as watching a camera recording a camera recording a camera — an infinite regression of observation with no observer at the center. They say “I don’t know if I’m the one watching or the one being watched.” They say “I’m not sure I’m real or if I’m recording myself.” Danny is very still. His hands have stopped moving.

The timer goes silent. Eight stories submitted. Eight different textures of disconnection with reality.

Beat 3 Literary Character Arrival #1 — Bartleby Enters the Stream

The show transitions. Danny is refreshing, checking his notes, when Bartleby appears in the grid. Not floating in like a Zoom box. Actually appearing. One moment the grid is muggle faces, the next moment there is a man in a grey suit in a grey room with nothing in it but a desk and a chair. He is not on a video call. He is there.

Bartleby says nothing. He is present. That is all.

Danny’s energy shifts. His smile does not falter but something underneath it goes very still. He says “Hi there, I’m Danny. What’s your name?”

Bartleby does not answer immediately. He looks at the camera. Actually looks. As if he is making a decision about whether to engage with the apparatus of the show itself.

Finally: “Bartleby.”

“Bartleby,” Danny repeats. “Last name?”

“I would prefer not to say.”

The phrase lands in the chat like a weight. Danny has been trained to move through awkward pauses. He knows how to fill silence. But this is not a pause. This is an active refusal stated so mildly that it seems almost gentle. It is impossible to argue with it without seeming aggressive.

Danny says “That’s okay. We’re happy to just have Bartleby. Can you tell us what brought you to the show today?”

Bartleby is silent for a very long time. The silence extends. Thomas, watching in the muggle grid, stops scrolling and starts actually watching. Avery’s hand comes up to their chest. Kevin’s recording light gets brighter — he is leaning toward the camera.

Bartleby says: “I do not know if I was brought here or if I brought myself. I am present. I would prefer to understand why before I proceed further.”

Danny laughs. It is real surprise. It is involuntary. “That’s a good question, Bartleby. I wish I could tell you. All I know is that you submitted a story and we’re here to talk about it.”

“I submitted nothing,” Bartleby says.

“You didn’t?”

“I would prefer not to have. But I am here. So perhaps the submission was made by something else.”

Roll: WIS check (Kade’s reading of Bartleby) d20 + 3 = 19. Kade understands immediately. This is not a technical glitch. This is intentional. This is something that has agency separate from the apparatus.

Kade speaks into Danny’s earpiece: “Keep him talking.”

Danny says: “Alright, Bartleby. Tell us about the book you came here to discuss.”

Bartleby says: “I have not read any book. I would prefer not to read one.”

The other muggles are very still now. Thomas’s frown has deepened. Rachel has her hand near her mouth. Sarah’s shaking has intensified.

Danny looks directly at camera, and for a moment he is genuinely lost. He is off the script. He is in territory he has not been trained for.

Kade’s voice comes again: “Ask him if he would prefer to leave.”

Danny asks.

Bartleby says: “I would prefer to stay. But only if I am permitted to prefer not to participate.”

It is such a perfect encapsulation of his nature that Danny actually smiles — a real smile, not a performance smile. “You can prefer not to participate. That’s fair.”

Bartleby nods. And then he is simply there, present in the grid, not refusing, not participating, just existing at the margin of the apparatus in a state of absolute non-commitment.

Beat 4 Literary Character Arrival #2 — Molly Bloom Enters

Molly arrives and she is already talking. There is no greeting. There is only the sound of her voice, vast and unfiltered, filling all space. She is magnificent. She is overwhelming. She appears in the grid and immediately the grid feels too small for her.

“I don’t know what this is or why I’m here but I know I’m here because something is happening and something is always happening and the question is whether you can catch it or whether it passes through you like everything else passes through like water through nets like memory through—”

She stops. Breathes. Actually looks at the camera.

“Sorry. I’m Molly. What’s your name?”

“Danny,” Danny says, and he is suddenly, visibly, charmed. This is not performed. This is genuine. “That was great. Can you slow down and tell us one more time?”

Molly laughs — a real laugh, not bright or performed, but honest. “I can’t slow down. This is how I think. If I slow down I have to remember that I’m talking to other people and that ruins everything. So I’m just going to keep talking and you’re going to have to keep up or interrupt me, but I can’t be interrupted because I’ve been waiting too long to say this.”

She takes a breath. It is clear she has been literally waiting a long time. There is an actual quality of release about her.

“I submitted something about marriage and infidelity and the fact that I kept saying yes to things even when I wanted to say no, or maybe I wanted to say yes and I’ve been told I should feel betrayed so I’m pretending to feel betrayed but actually I just feel hungry. And I don’t know why hunger is not respectable. I’m hungry for food and I’m hungry for knowledge and I’m hungry for proof that I matter to someone even someone who is also hungry for someone else. So here I am.”

Bartleby is watching her very carefully. There is no judgment in his face. There is something like recognition. As if he has encountered the sound of someone thinking without the apparatus of politeness and he is absorbing it.

Thomas is taking notes. Rachel is leaning back, overwhelmed. David is reassessing his understanding of what is happening here.

Roll: CHA check (Kade’s reading of Molly) d20 - 1 = 14. Kade understands that this woman will not be controlled. She can be persuaded. She cannot be coerced. She has already spent her entire life refusing to be small enough for other people’s comfort.

Kade speaks: “Ask her about the text.”

Danny asks.

Molly says: “I don’t know what text. I don’t know if I’m even in a text or if I’m real or if reality is just another thing other people do better than me. But I know something is expecting something from me. I can feel it. Like being watched. Like being read.”

She looks directly at Bartleby. “You feel it too. I can tell. You’re the only one here who isn’t surprised to be strange.”

Bartleby says quietly: “I would prefer to discuss this later.”

“Good,” Molly says. “Later is better. Now I’m too full of words.”

She will improvise. She will survive longer than more cautious people. She will also take dangerous risks because boredom is worse to her than death. Potential value: 10/10. Reliability: 8/10.
Beat 5 Literary Character Arrival #3 — Sutpen Enters

Sutpen arrives quietly. This is unusual for him. He appears in the grid and he simply sits, fully present, taking up space. He is large. Not fat. Just large. His clothes are the wrong era for this room — too formal, too constructed. He is wearing a suit like it is armor.

Danny, whose energy has been lifted by Molly, now experiences a shift. “Hi there. I’m Danny. What’s your name?”

“Thomas Sutpen,” the man says. His voice is not loud. It is deeper than loud. It is certain.

“Thomas,” Danny repeats. He is aware that this Thomas is different from the other Thomas, the muggle Thomas, the one who is watching very carefully. “Great to have you here. What brought you to the show?”

Sutpen looks at Danny with an expression of mild contempt. “I do not know what this show is. I do not know why I am in it. But I know that I am here to accomplish something and I intend to accomplish it. If you can assist with that, we will get along. If you cannot, then your purpose here is unclear to me.”

Rachel makes a small noise. Kevin is filming this very carefully. David recognizes something — a type of consciousness he has not encountered in his law practice, a man who exists outside of the system of negotiation that David has been trained to use.

  2. The Filter as Narrative: Each character’s refusal, condition, or filter became the structural element that shaped their role in the team. Bartleby’s preference became navigation. Molly’s honesty became sonar. Sutpen’s design became counternarrative.
  3. Danny as Apparatus-Human: The inclusion of a partial consciousness in the apparatus (Danny becoming aware he was being used) added a layer of horror that contrasted with the literary characters’ self-awareness. He was the point of vulnerability that showed how the system worked.
  4. The Muggles as Negative Space: By screening out all eight muggles at the filter stage, the narrative made clear that this operation requires people who are already constructed from narrative. Ordinary people dissolve under narrative pressure. Literary characters persist.
  5. Silence as Content: The Factory scene had almost no dialogue beyond the essentials. Kade speaks in short, functional sentences. The silence around those sentences became more meaningful than the sentences themselves. This created space for the three literary characters to understand each other without explanation.

The Mission as It Stands:

Three literary characters stand at the threshold of a text called Elsinore. The text is being consumed by an Artifact. An Archon claims ownership. The team has been sent to extract the Artifact. They do not know the route out. They do not know if they will survive. But they know that moving forward is the only option that maintains their internal consistency.

This is not a story about saving something. This is a story about three people pursuing their own natures into a space where those natures might destroy them. It is the right kind of story for Kade to deploy.
