# THE BIG SHOW — Phase 3 Template

## The Main Mission Encounter

---

## WHAT THIS IS

The Big Show is what everything has been building toward. The team has been assembled (recruiting), they've prepared (shopping trip), and now they execute the mission. This is where the game's core loop lives — characters entering a book world, facing opposition, making choices under pressure, and coming out changed.

This template generates a complete mission encounter with set pieces, NPCs, atmospheric effects, complications, and plot twists. It uses the hybrid resolution model: d20 for skill checks, narrative for battles and social encounters, thematic resonance as a force that shapes outcomes.

This file is usable standalone or as the final phase of a multi-phase session generated by `session-builder.md`.

---

## INPUTS

These values come from the Session Builder or from previous phase outputs. If running standalone, generate or specify them directly.

```
TEAM:               [character cards — from recruiting/shopping trip or generate fresh]
OPERATOR:           [same operator, or None — may or may not be present during the mission]
MISSION_TARGET:     [what the team is after]
OPPOSITION:         [who or what stands in the way]
COMPLICATION:       [the wrench in the plan]
INFO_ASYMMETRY:     [what the team knows and doesn't]
TARGET_BOOK:        [the book world the mission takes place in — specified or generated]
BEAT_COUNT:         [20 / 30 / 50 / custom — default: 30]
TONE:               [Literary / Gothic / Comedic / Thriller / Absurdist / Mixed]
ARTIFACTS:          [what the team carries from the shopping trip, or generated]
RELATIONSHIPS:      [carry forward from previous phases, or start fresh]
PLOT_TWISTS:        [None / 1 / Multiple / Specified]
SET_PIECES:         [Generated / Specified / From Prop House]
```

---

## STEP 1 — BUILD THE BOOK WORLD

The target book is the mission's environment. It's not a backdrop — it's a living system with its own rules, inhabitants, immune responses, and narrative gravity.

### If TARGET_BOOK is specified:

Research or generate the book world based on the actual text:
- **The World** — what does it look like, feel like, smell like? The physical reality of being inside this story.
- **The Rules** — what are the narrative laws of this book? Realism? Magical realism? Allegory? The rules of the book constrain what's possible inside it.
- **The Inhabitants** — NPCs native to the text. They belong here. The team doesn't.
- **The Immune System** — how does the book respond to foreign elements? Does it try to absorb them into the narrative? Reject them? Rewrite them? Ignore them? The book's immune response should be proportional to its Readership (popular books have stronger immune systems).
- **The Target Location** — where in the book is the mission target? How do you get there from the entry point?

### If TARGET_BOOK is random:

Generate a book world from scratch. Include:
- Title and author (can be real or invented — if real, the book should be from Project Gutenberg's catalog)
- A 2-3 sentence description of the world
- The world's rules, inhabitants, immune system, and target location as above

### Entry Vector

How does the team get in? Choose or generate from `tables/settings.md` (Big Show Approaches):

| Approach | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Through the front | Enter the text as it wants to be read. Follow the narrative in. Immersive but the book knows you're there. |
| Through the margins | Slip in through annotations, footnotes, editorial apparatus. Sneaky but fragile — one misstep and the text rejects you. |
| Through a connection | Enter via a linked text and cross over at a point of shared reference. Indirect and unpredictable. |
| Through a wound | Enter through a place where the text has been damaged, redacted, or corrupted. The gap is the door. Violent and unstable. |

## STEP 2 — BUILD SET PIECES

Set pieces are the landmark moments of the mission — scenes that are architecturally designed rather than emergently generated. Generate 3-5 set pieces that the mission will pass through. Not all need to be used; they're available for the narrative to reach.

Each set piece needs:

```
### [SET PIECE NAME]

**Location:** Where in the book world this happens.
**What's here:** The physical/narrative environment.
**The challenge:** What the team faces — combat, puzzle, social encounter, environmental hazard, moral dilemma.
**Resolution type:** d20 skill check / narrative / thematic / hybrid
**Stakes:** What happens if they succeed. What happens if they fail.
**Atmosphere:** Sensory details, mood, what makes this moment distinct.
```

Rules for set pieces:
- At least one should test the team's physical capabilities
- At least one should test their social or intellectual capabilities
- At least one should test their moral alignment or internal cohesion
- At least one should involve the book's immune system responding to their presence
- Set pieces should reward different character builds — not every challenge is solved by the strongest or smartest character

---

## STEP 3 — BUILD THE OPPOSITION

If OPPOSITION is a faction or individual, generate them in detail:

### Faction Opposition (Redactors, Auctioneers, etc.)
- **Roster:** 2-4 opposition characters with mini-profiles (name, role, 1-2 key traits, one notable ability)
- **Objective:** What do they want with the target?
- **Method:** How do they operate? Ambush? Negotiation? Sabotage? Race?
- **Advantage:** What do they have that the team doesn't?
- **Vulnerability:** What weakness can the team exploit?

### Environmental Opposition (the text itself, an Archon)
- **Nature:** How does it manifest? What does hostility look like in this context?
- **Escalation:** How does the opposition intensify over the course of the mission?
- **Communication:** Can it be reasoned with? Does it have demands? Or is it purely reactive?

### Rival Operator Opposition
- Generate a full character card for the rival operator
- Their team (if they have one) gets mini-profiles
- Define the information asymmetry — what does each side know about the other?

## STEP 4 — PLANT PLOT TWISTS

If PLOT_TWISTS is not "None," generate and embed twists into the mission. Twists should be:

- **Foreshadowed** — clues exist in earlier beats that only make sense in retrospect
- **Character-driven** — the twist should activate or challenge specific character conditions, reputations, or relationships
- **Consequential** — the twist changes what the team has to do, not just what they know

Twist categories:
- **The mission is not what it seems** — the target is different than described, the opposition is unexpected, the complication is worse than reported
- **A team member is compromised** — a condition activates, a reputation surfaces, a past connection to the target book or opposition is revealed
- **The book world changes** — the text rewrites itself, the immune system escalates, a narrative event occurs that reshapes the environment
- **The operator lied** — (if applicable) the mission parameters were incomplete or deliberately misleading

Plant the twist trigger at the appropriate beat. The twist should fire between 50-75% of the way through the encounter.

## STEP 5 — RUN THE ENCOUNTER

Run BEAT_COUNT beats.

### Pacing Structure

| Beats | Phase | What Happens |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 1-15% | **Entry** | Team enters the book world. The environment is established. First sensory impressions. The book's immune system stirs. |
| 15-30% | **Reconnaissance** | Team explores, gathers information, encounters the first signs of opposition. The world pushes back. |
| 30-50% | **Engagement** | First set pieces fire. The team faces challenges and makes choices. Skills are tested. Relationships are strained or strengthened. |
| 50-70% | **Escalation** | The opposition intensifies. Plot twists fire. The mission gets harder. The book world responds to the team's presence. Characters are tested individually and as a unit. |
| 70-85% | **Climax** | The final set piece. The team confronts the core challenge — the target, the opposition, or both simultaneously. Resolution is hybrid: d20 for mechanics, narrative for drama, thematic resonance as tiebreaker. |
| 85-95% | **Extraction** | Getting out. The book world may not want to let them leave. The route out may not be the route in. Casualties, costs, and consequences become clear. |
| 95-100% | **Aftermath** | The team is out. What did they gain? What did they lose? What changed? |

### Resolution Mechanics

**Skill Checks (d20):**
- When a character attempts something with mechanical uncertainty, roll d20 + relevant modifier
- Three tiers: Critical success (18+), Standard success (10-17), Failure (9 or below)
- Passive skills fire automatically on trigger conditions
- Active skills fire when the character would reasonably choose them
- Costs on success are paid even on critical successes
- Record all rolls

**Narrative Resolution:**
- Social encounters, moral dilemmas, and thematic conflicts resolve through narrative
- Character traits, conditions, filters, and relationships drive the outcome
- The LLM weighs: thematic resonance, internal consistency, character arcs, dramatic logic
- Narrative resolution should feel earned, not arbitrary

**Thematic Resonance:**
- When a character's nature aligns with the challenge — when the thing they are is exactly what the moment needs — give them advantage (roll twice, take the better result)
- When a character's nature contradicts the challenge — when who they are makes this harder — give them disadvantage (roll twice, take the worse result)
- Thematic resonance is not plot armor. A character who is perfectly suited for a moment can still fail. But the story notices the alignment.

### The Book World as Active Environment

The book world is not passive scenery. It responds to the team:

- **Immune response** — the text pushes back against foreign elements. This can manifest as narrative gravity (trying to pull characters into the book's plot), environmental hostility (weather, terrain, obstacles), or inhabitant suspicion (NPCs sense something is wrong about these strangers).
- **Narrative pressure** — the book's plot is happening whether the team is there or not. Events from the story occur around them. They may have to navigate through the book's own narrative while pursuing their mission.
- **Absorption attempts** — the book may try to make team members into characters in its story. Characters with conditions or backgrounds that resonate with the book are most vulnerable. A character from a tragedy entering another tragedy might feel the pull to stay.

### Character Behavior Rules

Same as previous phases, plus:
- **Characters under pressure reveal hidden conditions.** Semi-private conditions surface during the big show. Private conditions may surface under extreme stress.
- **Filters interact with the book world.** A character whose filter makes people remember their losses will affect the book's inhabitants, potentially disrupting the narrative.
- **Relationships formed in earlier phases matter under fire.** Characters who bonded protect each other. Characters who clashed may fail to cooperate when it matters. Characters who were indifferent make choices that reveal where they actually stand.
- **The operator (if present) has different behavior during the mission than during recruiting.** In recruiting, they were in control. In the field, they're in danger too. How they handle the loss of control is character-revealing.

### Beat-Close Rules (EVERY BEAT)

**Track 1 — Relationships:**
- Update Affinity/Aversion for all pairs who interacted
- Pressure creates different dynamics — trust tested under fire, dependence, resentment, sacrifice

**Track 2 — Mission Status:**
- After each beat, assess:
  - Mission progress (how close to the target?)
  - Team condition (injuries, condition changes, morale)
  - Opposition status (how close is the opposition? What do they know?)
  - Book world status (immune response level, narrative pressure, absorption risk)

### Narrative Rules

- Write in present tense.
- The book world is a character. Its prose style, its visual language, its emotional register should bleed into the narration. A mission inside *Wuthering Heights* should feel different from a mission inside *Journey to the West*.
- Combat follows the "graphic novel constraint" — terse, panel-thinking, visually composed. Think Alan Moore, not Tolkien.
- Death and loss are possible. Not every mission succeeds. Not every team member comes back. If the dice and the narrative say someone falls, let them fall.
- The opposition should be competent. They have their own plan, their own skills, their own reasons. They are not obstacles — they are characters.

### Sympathy Bias Guard

The big show is where bonding feels most earned — shared danger creates genuine connection. But check for:
- Does the opposition get humanized, or are they just obstacles?
- Do characters who should be in conflict still cooperate seamlessly?
- Does anyone make a selfish choice? Fear-driven choice? Wrong choice?
- Does the resolution feel too clean? Too satisfying? The best missions leave something unresolved.

## STEP 6 — OUTPUT

Produce one markdown file containing:

1. **Configuration Summary** — team, mission, book world, opposition
2. **Book World Profile** — the target book as a playable environment
3. **Set Piece Catalog** — all designed set pieces
4. **Opposition Profile** — the antagonist force in detail
5. **The Encounter** — all beats with narrative, rolls, and beat-close evaluations
6. **Final Relationship Tables** — end-state for all characters
7. **Mission Debrief:**
   - Mission outcome (success/failure/partial/pyrrhic)
   - What was gained and what was lost
   - Casualties or condition changes
   - Opposition outcome (defeated/escaped/destroyed/recruited/absorbed)
   - Plot twists: how they fired, what they changed, whether they were foreshadowed properly
8. **Post-Encounter Notes:**
   - What surprised the simulation
   - Which set pieces fired and which were bypassed
   - Character arcs that emerged across the full session (if multi-phase)
   - Which filters, conditions, or skills had the most impact
   - Moments worth preserving in the Prop House
   - Hooks for future sessions
9. **Prop House Candidates** — flag components that should be extracted by the Disassembler: memorable settings, NPCs who deserve their own cards, complications worth reusing, set pieces worth filing, artifacts discovered, plot devices that worked
