# THE WEDNESDAY GROUP — Iteration 3

---

## MISSION BRIEF

**Target (Roll: 3):** A character trapped inside a book who is trying to escape into the wrong story. The character is a young soldier named Andrei — a minor figure from a sprawling war epic — who has realized he is fictional and is attempting to cross into a pastoral romance two shelves over. He believes that if he can enter the love story, he won't have to die in Chapter 41. He's wrong — the pastoral romance is a closed text, and forcing entry will collapse both narratives.

**Opposition (Roll: 1):** A rival bookhunter named Lisle Kade — faster, better funded, and operating without ethical constraints. Kade doesn't want to save Andrei or stabilize the texts. She wants to weaponize the breach point — a character crossing between texts creates a momentary structural vulnerability that Kade can exploit to extract rare textual material from both books simultaneously. She has a three-person team already in position.

**Complication (Roll: 6):** The target text — the war epic — is emotionally resonant to one of the team members. It will recognize their affinity and try to keep them, offering them a role in its narrative, a place that feels like belonging. The text isn't malicious. It's lonely. It's losing a character and it wants a replacement.

**Ginsfeld Knows (Roll: 1):** Everything about the target — Andrei's location, his motivation, the structural damage his escape attempt is causing, the timeline before collapse. Almost nothing about the opposition — he knows Kade exists, knows she's in the field, but doesn't know her team composition, her timeline, or her extraction method.

## Mission Parameters

- **Opposition:** Lisle Kade — rival bookhunter. Faster, better funded, no ethics. Wants to weaponize the breach point. Has a three-person team already positioned. Ginsfeld knows almost nothing about her capabilities or timeline.

## What He's Looking For

1. **The Persuader** — Someone who can convince Andrei to abandon his escape attempt and return to his narrative willingly. Force won't work — a character resisting their own story creates more structural damage when compelled. Andrei has to choose to go back. That requires someone who can make Chapter 41 feel bearable.
2. **The Shield** — Someone who can resist the text's emotional resonance. The war epic will try to recruit a replacement for Andrei. The Shield holds the team's commitment to the mission against the most seductive thing a text can offer: belonging.
3. **The Operator** — Someone who can handle Kade's team. Ginsfeld doesn't know what Kade has, so the Operator needs to be adaptable, tactically sharp, and willing to act in conditions of incomplete information. Possibly ruthless.

## Decision Criteria

- Competence, reliability, team chemistry, controllability
- Wildcard tolerance: one unpredictable member IF the other two are stable
- Dealbreaker: anyone who can't take direction, will betray for personal reasons, or whose baggage will detonate mid-job
- **Special concern for this mission:** The emotional resonance complication means at least one character will be vulnerable to the text's pull. Ginsfeld needs to identify WHO is vulnerable and decide whether to risk them or cut them.

## THE ENCOUNTER

### Setting

A basement room beneath the Metro Library. Fluorescent lights, one flickering near the back corner. Six folding chairs arranged in a rough circle on linoleum that was once cream-colored and is now the yellow of old paperbacks. A card table by the wall holds a coffee urn with a cracked plastic spigot, a sleeve of Styrofoam cups, a jar of powdered creamer, and a dish of sugar packets — half torn open and empty. A hand-lettered sign on the door reads: *"Feel like you are a character without a plot? We meet every third Wednesday at 9:00 PM sharp. Bring an open mind. No weapons. Free coffee. — Donald Ginsfeld, PhD, Antiquities."*

The room smells like mildew and institutional coffee. A heating vent rattles arrhythmically. A water stain on the ceiling looks like a question mark or a hook, depending on where you stand.

---

### BEAT 1 — The Room Before

Ginsfeld arrives early. He sets out the chairs — six, as always. Tests the coffee. Terrible, as always. He adjusts the sign-in clipboard: NAME and ONE WORD THAT DESCRIBES HOW YOU'RE FEELING.

He sits and reviews what he knows. Andrei — young, desperate, rational enough to attempt cross-textual migration but naive enough to think it would work. The war epic is mid-collapse; its narrative architecture is compensating for the missing character by stretching, and stretch marks in a text become structural faults. Timeline: forty-eight hours before the breach destabilizes both books. Maybe less.

And Kade is in the field. He knows she's there the way you know a storm is coming — pressure changes, secondhand reports, the absence of other operators in the area. She's faster than him. Better funded. And she doesn't care what breaks.

He needs three people. Fast.

The fluorescent light flickers. Ginsfeld watches it. He has done this seventeen times now. Eleven teams. Six came back intact.

**Beat-Close:** No interactions. Setup beat.

*Ginsfeld's Internal Assessment: Forty-eight hours. Kade is already positioned. I need speed, persuasion, and someone who can handle a hostile operator in unpredictable conditions. The emotional resonance complication is the wild card — I won't know who it hits until they're in the room and I can read them. The war epic is about duty, sacrifice, and the impossibility of desertion. Whoever resonates with THAT — whoever has spent their life trapped in a story they can't leave — they're my vulnerability. And possibly my best asset, if I can keep them on task.*

He sets the clipboard down gently — the gentleness itself a demonstration of controlled force. He looks at the coffee urn, pours a cup, and sits in the chair nearest the wall. Not hiding — positioning. Back to something solid.

His coat clinks faintly as he sits.

### BEAT 3 — Scheherazade Arrives

The door opens and a woman enters who makes the room feel like it has an audience.

Scheherazade is not striking in the way of great beauties — she is striking the way a voice is striking. Modest dress, dark hair covered loosely, hands that move when she talks, which she does immediately, to no one in particular and to everyone at once:

"What a marvelous room. It has the exact proportions of a story that wants to be told but hasn't found its narrator yet."

**CONDITION — Thread Voice (o2o):** Her voice fills the space. Not with volume — with texture. The cadence carries narrative even in a casual observation. Ginsfeld feels himself leaning forward. Valjean, in his wall chair, turns his head.

**FILTER — The Compulsion to Listen:** Both men feel it. The urge not to speak, not to respond, but to *wait* — to hear what comes next. As if she's already begun a story and interrupting would be a small violence.

She picks up the clipboard. NAME: *Scheherazade.* ONE WORD: *Listening.*

She doesn't take coffee. She sits in the chair opposite Valjean — across the circle, creating a diagonal of attention. She looks at Ginsfeld with eyes that are reading the room the way a musician reads a score.

**PASSIVE SKILL — Story Sense: d20 + 4 = 16 (rolled 12). DC 14 met — she reads the architecture of the situation.**

Her eyes move from the sign-in sheet to the chairs to the coffee to Ginsfeld's posture. The structure reveals itself: this is not a support group. It has the shape of an audition — a first act that introduces characters, a second act that tests them, and a third act she can't see yet but whose presence she can feel in the way Ginsfeld holds his pen. Like someone keeping score.

She doesn't say anything about it. She sits. She listens. Her expression says she'll wait to see what kind of story this is before deciding what role to play in it.
