# THE WEDNESDAY GROUP — Iteration 2

---

## MISSION BRIEF

**Target (Roll: 7):** A book that is being read by someone inside it, creating a recursive loop. The text is *The Circular Ruins* by an unknown author — a narrative about a man dreaming another man into existence, except the dreamed man has begun reading the book that contains him, and each reading generates a new layer of reality nested inside the last. The recursion is now seven layers deep and accelerating.

**Opposition (Roll: 4):** The text itself, which is hostile and semi-aware. The recursive loop has given the book a kind of distributed consciousness — not intelligence exactly, but a fierce territorial reflex. It recognizes intrusions the way an immune system recognizes infection. Each layer of the recursion has its own defenses, and they coordinate.

**Complication (Roll: 3):** The extraction can only happen during a specific event inside the book's narrative — the moment when the dreamed man looks up from the page and sees fire. This event occurs once per cycle of the recursion, and the window lasts approximately ninety seconds of subjective time.

**Ginsfeld Knows (Roll: 4):** That his last team tried this job and didn't come back. Three people. Experienced. He found their equipment at the entry point — packs, tools, notes — but no bodies and no signal. The book was three layers shallower when they went in. It's been growing since.

## What He's Looking For

1. **The Anchor** — Someone with extraordinary endurance and psychological stability who can hold the team's position in deep recursion without losing themselves. The deeper layers will try to absorb anyone inside them. He needs someone who won't dissolve.
2. **The Reader** — Someone with the intelligence and pattern-recognition to navigate a hostile, self-aware text. They need to read the recursion's structure, anticipate its defenses, and find the extraction window.
3. **The Closer** — Someone who can act decisively in a ninety-second window under extreme pressure. Speed, nerve, and the willingness to commit to an irreversible action.
4. *(If he's lucky)* **The Talker** — Someone who might be able to negotiate with or distract a semi-aware text. Not required. Possibly impossible. But if the text is conscious enough to have defenses, it might be conscious enough to have preferences.

- Wildcard tolerance: one unpredictable member IF the other two are stable
- Dealbreaker: anyone who can't take direction, will betray for personal reasons, or whose baggage will detonate mid-job
- **Special concern for this mission:** self-destructive tendencies are a mortal risk. A text that feeds on consciousness will eat someone who's already trying to disappear.

## THE ENCOUNTER

### Setting

A basement room beneath the Metro Library. Fluorescent lights, one flickering near the back corner. Six folding chairs arranged in a rough circle on linoleum that was once cream-colored and is now the yellow of old paperbacks. A card table by the wall holds a coffee urn with a cracked plastic spigot, a sleeve of Styrofoam cups, a jar of powdered creamer, and a dish of sugar packets — half of them torn open and empty. A hand-lettered sign on the door reads: *"Feel like you are a character without a plot? We meet every third Wednesday at 9:00 PM sharp. Bring an open mind. No weapons. Free coffee. — Donald Ginsfeld, PhD, Antiquities."*

The room smells like mildew and institutional coffee. A heating vent rattles arrhythmically. There is a water stain on the ceiling shaped like a question mark or a hook, depending on where you're standing.

### BEAT 1 — The Room Before

Ginsfeld arrives twenty minutes early. He sets out the chairs — six, though he expects five. The extra is for whoever didn't read the sign carefully enough, or for the universe's habit of sending uninvited guests. He tests the coffee. It's terrible. He adds more creamer. It becomes terrible in a different way.

He straightens the chairs. Adjusts the angle of the sign-in sheet — a clipboard with a ballpoint pen on a string. The sheet asks for NAME and ONE WORD THAT DESCRIBES HOW YOU'RE FEELING. He's found that the word people choose tells him more than an hour of conversation. He has done this fourteen times. He has recruited nine teams. Two came back intact.

He sits. He breathes. He does not think about the last team.

The fluorescent light flickers. Ginsfeld looks at it, then away.

**Beat-Close Evaluations:**

No interactions to evaluate. Setup beat.

*Ginsfeld's Internal Assessment: The mission requires depth — literal and psychological. Whoever walks through that door, I need to see if they can hold together when reality starts nesting. Three people went in four weeks ago. The text was four layers deep. Now it's seven. The math is simple: it's growing faster than we can plan. I need an anchor, a reader, and someone with the nerve to move in a ninety-second window. What I'll probably get is four people with problems. Let's see whose problems are useful.*

### BEAT 2 — Väinämöinen Arrives

The door opens slowly. Not hesitantly — slowly, with the deliberation of someone who has never in his life been in a hurry. Väinämöinen enters. He is old in a way that makes the word insufficient. White beard to his sternum, hands like driftwood, a heavy wool coat that smells faintly of pine and frozen water. His eyes are the color of lake ice — pale, clear, and very far away.

The temperature in the room drops two degrees. Not dramatically. Just enough that Ginsfeld notices. The condensation on the coffee urn thickens.

Väinämöinen looks at the room. He looks at the chairs. He looks at the sign. He looks at Ginsfeld. His expression is not unfriendly. It is the expression of a man who has walked into rooms for longer than rooms have existed and knows they are all, fundamentally, the same room.

He picks up the clipboard. Under NAME, he writes *Väinämöinen*. The pen looks like a twig in his gnarled fingers. Under ONE WORD, after a pause, he writes *Continuing*.

He sits in the chair farthest from the door. He does not take coffee. He folds his hands in his lap and waits with the stillness of geography.
