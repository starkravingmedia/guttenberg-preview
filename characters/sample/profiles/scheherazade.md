## Description

The woman who defeated a king with cliffhangers. Scheherazade tells stories the way other people breathe — not because she wants to, but because if she stops, she dies. Every tale nests another tale, every ending opens a door, every silence is a negotiation. She is exhausted in a way that makes her more dangerous, not less. She fights with unfinished sentences.

## Conditions

### The Unfinished Sentence
- **Duration:** Permanent
- **Visibility:** Public
- **Observability:** o2o

She never finishes. Not stories, not sentences, not gestures. Every statement trails off into implication, every answer opens a question. This is not a habit — it is survival architecture. Those who spend time with her find themselves leaning forward, waiting for conclusions that never arrive. The anticipation is the weapon.

### The Exhaustion of a Thousand Nights
- **Duration:** Permanent
- **Visibility:** Semi-private
- **Observability:** o2o

She has been talking for a very long time. The fatigue is bone-deep but she has learned to wear it like jewelry. Perceptive observers notice the pauses are getting fractionally longer, the metaphors slightly less ornate. She is rationing her imagination. The supply is not infinite. It only has to outlast the listener.

### The Alphabetist's Fantasy
- **Duration:** Until she finds a story simple enough to rest inside
- **Visibility:** Private
- **Observability:** no2o

She fantasizes about a world simple enough to fit in an alphabet. A is for apple. B is for ball. No nested narratives, no framing devices, no stories within stories within stories. She took the Child's Primer in the equipment draft because she wanted to hold something with an ending. She has not told anyone this.

### Narrative Gravity
- **Duration:** Permanent
- **Visibility:** Public
- **Observability:** o2o

Stories bend toward her. In any room, conversations drift from logistics to narrative. People start telling her things they didn't intend to say. Facts reshape themselves into anecdotes. She does not do this on purpose. It happens the way gravity happens — the mass is just there.

## Reputations

### "The Woman Who Would Not Stop Talking"
- **Source:** The Lamplighters (widespread)

The Lamplighters consider Scheherazade the single most effective intelligence asset they've never managed to recruit. She acquires secrets the way other people acquire small talk. The problem is extraction — she never gives a straight answer, only stories that contain the answer somewhere inside them. Three Lamplighter analysts have been assigned full-time to parsing her output. One of them has started writing poetry.

### "The Defensive Nightmare"
- **Source:** The Redactors (moderate)

The Redactors tried to erase her once. The operation is classified but the outcome is not: the three-person strike team returned with the job undone and a shared conviction that the story wasn't finished yet. They couldn't explain what story. Two of them requested reassignment. The third is still writing the mission report, which is now forty pages long and contains a subplot about a fisherman.

### "The News Cycle"
- **Source:** The Dockhands (limited)

A few Dockhands who move cargo between texts have noticed that narratives passing through Scheherazade's vicinity arrive at their destinations changed. Not corrupted — elaborated. A simple fable gains a framing device. A love story acquires a narrator who comments on the love story. The Dockhands have filed it as a shipping anomaly. The paperwork is getting recursive.

## Roll Skills

### And Then... `Active`
- **Trigger:** Scheherazade chooses to delay, redirect, or suspend a situation by embedding it in narrative
- **Mechanic:** d20 + CHA (+4)

| Roll  | Outcome |
|-------|---------|
| 18+   | The situation suspends completely. Everyone involved forgets what they were about to do and becomes absorbed in the story. Time effectively stops while the narrative continues. She can hold this as long as her stamina allows. |
| 10-17 | The situation slows. Urgency drains from the room. Opponents hesitate, allies buy time, and the story provides cover for repositioning. The delay is real but not total. |
| 1-9   | The story doesn't land. The audience isn't hooked. Worse, the failed narrative creates an opening — the silence after a bad story is the most dangerous silence there is. |

- **Cost:** Every story she tells draws from a finite well. She doesn't know how deep the well is but she can feel the water level dropping. Overuse leaves her narratively depleted — her speech flattens, her metaphors thin, and for brief terrible moments she sounds like someone giving a report instead of someone casting a spell.

### The Nested Hook `Passive`
- **Trigger:** Fires when someone attempts to end a conversation, leave her presence, or conclude an interaction before she's ready
- **Mechanic:** d20 + CHA (+4)

| Roll  | Outcome |
|-------|---------|
| 18+   | The departing person physically cannot leave. Not through force — through compulsion. The story has them. They will stay until it's done, and it is never done. They don't resent this. That's the terrifying part. |
| 10-17 | The person pauses at the threshold. They stay a little longer. The delay is brief but in narrative combat, brief is enough. |
| 1-9   | The hook fails. The person leaves and takes with them an unfinished story that will fester. An incomplete Scheherazade narrative is an unpredictable thing — it may resolve itself in ways she didn't intend. |

- **Cost:** The hook binds both ways. Every person she snags with an unfinished story becomes a thread she has to maintain. Too many open threads and her narrative architecture starts to buckle. She becomes a hostage to her own cliffhangers.

## Filter

### "The Story That Hasn't Ended Yet"

In Scheherazade's presence, everything becomes a story. Conversations acquire narrative structure without anyone intending it. People start speaking in beginnings, middles, and setups-without-punchlines. Facts soften into anecdotes. Arguments develop subplots. The room feels like the middle of a book you can't put down — not because it's good, but because you need to know what happens next. Leaving feels like closing a novel mid-chapter. You can do it. But something in you protests.
