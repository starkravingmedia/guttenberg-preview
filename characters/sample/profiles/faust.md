## Description

Scholar, overreacher, deal-maker, damned. Faust sold his soul for knowledge and got everything except the ability to stop wanting more. He knows the rules of every game because he's read the contract. He knows the cost of every move because he's already paying. He speaks in the calm, measured tone of a man who signed something terrible a long time ago and has made peace with the installments.

## Conditions

### The Clause
- **Duration:** Permanent
- **Visibility:** Semi-private
- **Observability:** o2o

Somewhere in the fine print of existence, Faust owes a debt that compounds. He doesn't know when it will be called. Others with sufficient perception feel a faint contractual weight around him — not evil, exactly, but encumbered. Like standing next to someone carrying an invisible suitcase full of lead.

### The Scholar's Hunger
- **Duration:** Permanent
- **Visibility:** Public
- **Observability:** o2o

He cannot stop learning. Every new system, every hidden mechanism, every locked door is a compulsion. This is not curiosity — it is appetite. Others notice because he leans forward at exactly the moments when normal people lean back. When someone says "you don't want to know," his pupils dilate.

### Mephistopheles' Shadow
- **Duration:** Until the debt is settled
- **Visibility:** Private
- **Observability:** no2o

In moments of extreme stress or triumph, Faust catches a second shadow at the edge of his vision — one that moves half a beat too late and smiles when he doesn't. No one else can see it. He has stopped being alarmed by it. That, perhaps, is the most alarming thing.

## Reputations

### "The Man Who Signed"
- **Source:** The Bindery (widespread)

The Bindery knows about the contract. They know it was signed in something that wasn't quite ink. They respect the audacity and fear the implications — a man who would sign his soul away is either the bravest person in the room or the most dangerous. The Bindery tends to offer him excellent terms on lesser bargains, hoping to stay on whatever side of his ledger survives.

### "The Professor"
- **Source:** The Lamplighters (moderate)

The Lamplighters have noted Faust's extraordinary ability to decode systems, translate between incompatible frameworks, and find the structural weaknesses in any text he enters. They've offered him work three times. He declined each time — politely, in language that suggested he was declining on someone else's behalf. The Lamplighters are still deciding whether that someone is real or a negotiating fiction.

### "Sulfur and Quill-Ink"
- **Source:** The Ashkeepers (limited)

A few Ashkeepers have reported a faint smell of sulfur in texts recently visited by someone matching Faust's description. They don't know his name yet. They've filed it under environmental anomalies. The file is getting thicker.

## Roll Skills

### Read the Contract `Active`
- **Trigger:** Faust chooses to analyze a system, agreement, or hidden mechanism
- **Mechanic:** d20 + INT (+5)

| Roll  | Outcome |
|-------|---------|
| 18+   | He sees the entire architecture — not just the rules, but who wrote them and what they were afraid of. He identifies the clause that was meant to stay hidden. The system is transparent to him. |
| 10-17 | He reads the system accurately — its structure, its constraints, its primary exploit. He knows what it does and roughly what it costs. |
| 1-9   | He reads what the system wants him to read. The false front. He proceeds with unearned confidence, which is more dangerous than ignorance. |

- **Cost:** Every system he decodes remembers being decoded. Texts he has analyzed are subtly harder to re-enter — their immune systems learn his signature. The more he reads, the more the literary world knows his methods.

### Call It Due `Passive`
- **Trigger:** Fires when Faust makes a promise, offer, or agreement of any kind — including casual ones
- **Mechanic:** d20 + CHA (+3)

| Roll  | Outcome |
|-------|---------|
| 18+   | The agreement carries binding force beyond the social. Both parties feel the weight of the terms settling into reality. Breaking this agreement will have consequences neither side can predict. |
| 10-17 | The agreement lands with unusual gravity. The other party takes it more seriously than they intended to. Faust's word feels expensive. |
| 1-9   | The contractual energy misfires. Faust accidentally binds himself to terms he didn't intend, or the other party senses the mechanism and recoils. |

- **Cost:** Every agreement Faust makes — even a casual "I'll be there" — accumulates on his ledger. The original debt notices. Each new contract, however minor, increases the probability that the Clause activates. He is, in effect, paying compound interest on borrowed time every time he shakes hands.

## Filter

### "The Price of Everything"

In Faust's presence, every interaction becomes faintly transactional. Not mercenary — something deeper. People near him become aware of what things cost. Not in money, but in time, in loyalty, in the parts of themselves they trade away without noticing. Conversations acquire subtext about value. Generosity feels strategic. Gifts feel like opening offers. The air around Faust smells, very faintly, of ink drying on a contract no one remembers signing.
