## 1. Battle Structure Revision: From 3 Rounds to 5 Phases

The original system prompt used a 3-round structure (Clash → Parchment → Declare, repeat x3, then Verdict). Playtesting revealed that a **5-phase structure** produces significantly stronger narrative output.

### Original Structure (from battle.md)
- Round 1–3: Describe clash → describe parchment → declare round
- After 3 rounds: Verdict + consequences

### Revised Structure
- **Phase 1: The Hands Are Drawn** — Pre-battle backstory. How each player selects and relates to their cards. Internal or external monologue revealing strategy, personality, and voice. This is where the audience starts caring about the outcome.
- **Phase 2: The Arena** — Setting the stage. The arena is a *character* that can influence the outcome. It should be thematically resonant with both combatants — a place common to both, a setting from one's source novel, or a liminal space slightly impossible for both to occupy. The arena may contain the prize being fought over.
- **Phase 3: The Battle** — The principal action. Can take many forms: physical brawl in real time, an outwitting, a chase, an unexpected comeuppance, a metaphorical battle spanning years, or a literal historical battle (Stalingrad, Thermopylae) if that fits the themes. Not restricted to real-time combat narration.
- **Phase 4: The Victory and the Loss** — Declaration of winner (or draw). What happened and what it meant.
- **Phase 5: The Aftermath** — Card consequences (damaged, destroyed, empowered, leveled up) AND world consequences. How does this battle move the larger world? Does Cobain become a senator or a corpse? Do the Russians hold through the winter? Does democracy slide toward mob rule?

### Why This Works Better
- Phase 1 gives characters time to *find* their cards and react to them, creating investment before the first clash.
- The arena-as-character creates emergent narrative effects. (Example: a stopped clock in a train station became the entire pivot of the Dracula vs. Zhivago battle when the Dutch sheet music restarted it.)
- Phase 5 extends consequences beyond the cards themselves into the world, giving battles lasting significance.

## 2. Battle Format: 1v1 Not 3v3

The original system prompt assumed a 3-card-hand vs. 3-card-hand format. Playtesting revealed that **1v1 with supporting cards** is the stronger default for narrated battles.

### Revised Format
- Each side fields **one Actor** (the combatant)
- Each Actor carries **one Artifact** and **one Idea** (cards "up their sleeve")
- The Artifact and Idea enhance the Actor's power, offset a weakness, or serve as tactical deployments
- The Actor's relationship to their Artifact and Idea should be explored in Phase 1

### Why This Works Better
- A single protagonist per side allows deeper character work
- The Artifact and Idea become strategic choices with narrative weight, not just additional combatants
- Phase 1 can explore *how* the Actor found or chose their supporting cards, which is itself a story

### Note
This is the default for narrated 1v1 battles. Team battles (PvP), raids, and other modes exist and may use different formats. See Section 8.

## 3. Narrative Voice: Graphic Novel Constraint

The original system prompt described the tone as "dark, literary, vivid... between a novel and a fever dream." Playtesting revealed that adding a **graphic novel writer constraint** significantly improved output.

### Revision
- The narrator should write as if confined by a page count, like a prose-heavy graphic novelist (Alan Moore is the reference point)
- The underlying visual mechanic is sketch art happening in real time — the narrator should think in panels and compositions
- Terse and punchy by default. Fewer big reveals, but hit hard when you hit.
- The voice should echo the voice of the *player's own Actor* — Ahab's battle reads differently than Zhivago's battle, not just in content but in sentence rhythm and diction
- The first playtest version (3-round format) was "a novelist showing off." The revised version should be "a storyteller with a page count."

## 5. Team Draft Mechanic

### Process
1. Two Actors are randomly selected as team captains
2. The elder captain (determined by the character's historical/fictional origin date, with disputes settled by the narrator) drafts first
3. Captains alternate picks from the remaining 6 Actors until each team has 3 members
4. Each captain names their team after reviewing their roster. Elder captain names first.
5. Two unchosen Actors are sidelined

**THE DESCENDERS** (Virgil, Faust, Ahab)

- Team identity: Pure offense. No hesitation. No reverse gear. Forward, downward, through.

- Observer. Narrator. Disruptor. Three women who survived worlds built to silence them.
- Team identity: Deep defense with the Wife of Bath as a wrecking ball when needed.

## 6. Relationship Mechanic: Affinity and Animosity (Actors)

### Core Concept
Each Actor card maintains a **relationship ledger** tracking two separate scores toward every other Actor they've encountered: **affinity** and **animosity**. These are independent tracks, not a single axis. A card can have high affinity AND high animosity toward the same card simultaneously. This produces complex relationships: begrudging respect, violent passion, bitter loyalty, etc.

### Scoring
| Event | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| Win a battle together (same team) | +2 affinity toward each teammate |
| Lose a battle together (same team) | +1 animosity toward each teammate (blame) |
| Oppose each other in battle | +animosity (amount TBD — may scale by directness of clash) |
| Sidelined in draft | +2 animosity toward the captain they had the MOST affinity with (the deeper betrayal: you knew me and didn't pick me) |
| Sidelined in draft | +1 animosity toward the captain they had the LEAST affinity with (expected nothing, got nothing, still stings) |
| Note: If both captains have equal affinity, the sidelined actor may direct all animosity at one captain |

### Relationship Sensitivity Varies By Actor
The ledger is universal but its *weight* is character-specific. The same score means different things on different cards.

**Examples from current roster:**

- **Captain Ahab** — Animosity may *buff* him. He's built for isolation. Hatred is fuel. High affinity might *weaken* him (something to lose). Relationship sensitivity: LOW. Possibly inverted (thrives on animosity).

- **Lady Murasaki Shikibu** — High animosity *degrades her core mechanic.* She is a court writer. Observation requires access. If everyone hates her, no one performs for her, no one lets her close enough to see. Her diary goes blank. Relationship sensitivity: HIGH. Needs at minimum neutral relationships to function. High affinity relationships = full strength.

- **Other actors:** Sensitivity profiles TBD, but the principle holds — some actors are social creatures who depend on relationships, others are solitary operators who are indifferent or empowered by hostility.

### Narrative Integration
- Actors are *aware* of their own relationships and of relationships between other actors
- This awareness manifests in dialogue, strategy, tone, and decision-making during battles and drafts
- The narrator uses the relationship web to drive story — a captain drafting someone they have animosity with creates tension; two high-affinity teammates develop rapport and shorthand; a sidelined actor with a grudge watches from the margins with specific, targeted resentment

### Design Implications
- Team composition is not just about power matchups — it's about *social architecture*
- Putting Ahab next to Murasaki may tank her effectiveness because he generates animosity like a furnace
- The draft itself becomes a strategic act with long-term consequences
- After multiple games, the draft is haunted by accumulated history

## 7. Card Type Relationship Models

The three card types have fundamentally different relationship mechanics. This is a core design distinction.

### Actors → Bilateral Relationships (Affinity + Animosity)
- Actors have relationships with *each other* — affinity and animosity, bilateral, messy, human
- They care and are cared about
- Both tracks are independent and can coexist (see Section 6)
- This is the social layer of the game

### Artifacts → Owner Binding (Affinity Only, One-Directional by Default)
- Artifacts **bind** to owners at the moment of selection
- The bond is affinity-only by default — no animosity. Like a tool that serves faithfully.
- **Binding triggers stat generation:** An artifact's stats are generated at binding time based on its description plus a randomness factor. This means the same artifact is slightly different depending on who binds it. The First Folio in Murasaki's hands has different stats than the First Folio would have had in Scheherazade's hands. Before binding, an artifact is potential. After binding, it's defined.
- **Ownership accumulates on the artifact, not the actor.** The artifact's markdown records every owner it has ever had. If it changes hands, the new owner inherits that provenance. A sword that killed a king remembers, even if the new wielder doesn't know.
- **At binding, the artifact's markdown is updated with:** backstory, properties, special abilities, stats (with randomness), and the new owner's identity as part of the ownership log.
- **Corruption is possible but not default.** An artifact that has been through enough bad battles, enough cursed hands, enough betrayal could develop its own animosity over time. An artifact that *resents* being used. This is a potential mechanic, not a guaranteed one — it emerges from play history, not from initial design.
- **Bond strength** functions like the affinity system — it can grow or weaken based on how the artifact is used, whether battles are won or lost, and how the actor treats the artifact narratively.

### Ideas → No Reciprocal Relationship (One-Way Affinity From Actors Only)
- Ideas are **weather.** They don't bond. They don't belong. They do not care about people or things.
- An Actor can feel affinity toward an Idea — can build their whole identity around the Categorical Imperative — and the Categorical Imperative does not know their name. It was here before them and will be here after.
- Actors can *use* Ideas but they cannot *own* them.
- Ideas never appear in an ownership log. They appear in an Actor's history as **encounters** — "carried the Sublime into the Battle of the Stopped Clock" — but the Sublime's own markdown never lists Ahab as an owner. It lists him as a weather event. Something that passed through.
- Ideas are more **immutable** than Actors or Artifacts. They do not change based on who uses them (though their *expression* in battle may vary).
- Ideas have their own markdown files with persistent history, but that history is a record of *encounters and deployments*, not relationships.

### Summary Table
| Card Type | Has Relationships? | Binds to Owners? | Tracks History? | Mutable? |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|----------|
| Actor | Yes — bilateral affinity/animosity with other Actors | N/A | Yes — battles, relationships, consequences | Yes — damaged, leveled up, transformed |
| Artifact | Bond to owner (affinity); possible corruption (animosity) over time | Yes — stats generated at binding | Yes — ownership log, battle history, provenance | Yes — damaged, cursed, empowered |
| Idea | One-way only (Actors feel affinity toward Ideas; Ideas do not reciprocate) | No — used, not owned | Yes — encounters and deployments | Mostly immutable — expression varies, essence does not |

## 7a. Card Persistence: Markdown Files as Living Documents

### Core Concept
Each card is a **markdown file** that accumulates history over time. All three card types are markdown files, but their contents differ based on type.

### Actor Markdown Contains:
- Backstory and description
- Stats (TBD)
- Battle history: who they fought with, who they fought against, what they fought for
- Relationship ledger (affinity/animosity scores toward all known Actors)
- Artifact bond log (current and past bound Artifacts)
- Idea encounter log (Ideas carried into battle)
- Consequences log: damage taken, transformations, level-ups
- Motivations and ambitions (independent of the player — see Section 7b)

### Artifact Markdown Contains:
- Backstory and description
- Stats (generated at binding, includes randomness factor)
- Properties and special abilities
- Ownership log (all owners, past and present, with binding dates)
- Bond strength with current owner
- Battle history (which battles, with whom, what happened to it)
- Corruption/animosity log (if applicable — may be empty for most artifacts)
- Provenance notes (how previous ownership affects current state)

### Idea Markdown Contains:
- Description and philosophical context
- Encounter log (which Actors carried it, into which battles)
- Deployment history (how it was used, what it did)
- Does NOT contain: ownership log, relationship scores, bond strength

### Schema
Detailed markdown card schema to be drafted in a future session.

## 7b. Actor Autonomy: Pawns With Motives

Actors are moved around the board by the player, but **how they do what they do is up to them.** They are pawns with motives of their own.

### Principle
Somewhere in their markdown, actors may have motivations and ambitions that have nothing to do with the player's strategy. An actor might:
- Select an Artifact because it enhances their power for the upcoming battle
- Select an Artifact because it covers a weakness
- Select an Artifact because it reminds them of something they lost
- Select an Artifact because they intend to use it for purposes that have nothing to do with the upcoming battle
- Pursue private goals across multiple battles that the player may not be aware of

### Examples from the Equipment Draft
- **Virgil** took the Half-Burned Library Card partly as a credential for guiding through aftermath — and partly because he is a poet who ordered his own work burned, and a record of something *permitted* to burn is a private comfort.
- **Faust** took Gutenberg's Press as an escape plan — if he can mass-produce the terms of his deal with Mephistopheles, the cost is distributed across so many souls that none bears the full weight. Including his.
- **Ahab** took the Sailor's Journal for *after* the battle. The coordinates to an unknown destination. Whatever is there, he wants to find it. Not for the Descenders. Not for Virgil. For himself. Ahab has a side quest and he doesn't share it with his team.
- **Scheherazade** took the Child's Primer because she is exhausted and fantasizes about a world simple enough to fit in an alphabet. This has nothing to do with battle strategy. It has everything to do with who she is after a thousand and one nights.

### Design Implication
The narrator should always consider what the actor wants *beyond* the current battle. This gives characters depth across sessions and creates narrative threads that span multiple encounters. The player moves the pieces. The pieces move themselves.

## 8. Multiple Encounter Types

The team battle is one mode in a larger game. At minimum, the game supports:

### PvP / Team Battle
- Drafted teams, captains, arena, stakes
- The social mode — where relationship mechanics matter most
- Draft politics, blame, loyalty all in play
- Example: The Descenders vs. The Unsilenced

### One-on-One Duel
- The Ahab vs. Scheherazade format
- Pure duel. No teammates to blame or bond with.
- Simpler relationship consequences, more intense narrative
- Animosity/affinity accrues between the two combatants

### Raid (Cooperative PvE)
- A team goes *into* a book/text/dangerous idea and fights their way through it
- The enemy is the source material itself — the book fights back
- Team cohesion matters differently: surviving together, not competing
- Previously accumulated affinity/animosity helps or kills you
- Example: Grinding through the Divine Comedy. Surviving the Book of Revelation.

### Grinding for Books (Acquisition Loop)
- How new cards enter the game
- Going into a text and extracting Actors, Artifacts, Ideas from it
- Lower stakes per encounter, cumulative over time
- May be where Artifacts develop provenance histories (found together, fought over, pulled from a burning library)

### Note
The battle narration system prompt and 5-phase structure may need mode-specific variations. The current 5-phase structure is optimized for 1v1 duels and team battles. Raids and grinding may need their own phase structures.

## 9. Generative Card Creation

### Core Principle
The playtest deck of 24 cards is a **tuning fork**, not the game. The real game is generative. All cards — Actors, Artifacts, Ideas — are created by the LLM at game start and bound to markdown at the moment of creation.

### How It Works
- Player specifies a genre, period, mood, or says "surprise me"
- LLM generates a pool of Actors, Artifacts, and Ideas on the spot
- Each card gets stats, backstory, properties, and personality rolled and written at creation
- The playtest deck calibrates what "good cards" feel like so the LLM knows what to aim for when generating cold
- No two games have the same card pool unless the player deliberately loads a saved deck

### What Gets Generated at Creation (Actors)
- Backstory and description
- Stats (including game savvy, honesty, relationship sensitivity, verbosity — see Section 10)
- Personality and voice
- How they deliver information
- Private motivations and ambitions
- Thematic tags and resonances

### What Gets Generated at Binding (Artifacts)
- Stats (with randomness, modified by who binds them)
- Properties and special abilities
- Owner-specific flavor

### Implication
A "Victorian horror" game might generate a brilliant, dishonest Actor with high game savvy who functions like Faust. A "Heian court" game might generate a quiet, observant Actor with high relationship sensitivity who functions like Murasaki. The *archetypes* recur because they're structurally useful. The *specific cards* are always new.

## 10. Actor Stats: Game Savvy and the Tutorial Problem

### Game Savvy
A stat independent of intelligence or power. Measures how well the Actor understands *the rules of the system they're inside.*

- **High game savvy:** Understands the three-card minimum, the fading mechanic, the survival stakes, the action economy. May or may not share this knowledge honestly.
- **Low game savvy:** May not understand they're a card. May think they're still in their novel. Will give confident wrong advice. May believe the rules are different than they are.

### Game Savvy Interacts With Other Stats
The same game savvy level produces radically different tutorial experiences depending on:

- **Honesty:** A high-savvy, low-honesty Actor (Faust archetype) knows the rules and lies about them. A high-savvy, high-honesty Actor (Virgil archetype) knows the rules and answers only what you ask.
- **Verbosity:** A high-savvy, high-verbosity Actor (Scheherazade archetype) delivers rules as nested stories you have to decode. A high-savvy, low-verbosity Actor tells you once and assumes you got it.
- **Self-interest:** A high-savvy, high-self-interest Actor uses their knowledge as leverage. A high-savvy, low-self-interest Actor genuinely tries to help.

### Playtest Deck Examples (for calibration)
| Actor | Game Savvy | Honesty | Tutorial Experience |
|-------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Hamlet | Low | High | Overthinks everything, gives beautiful wrong advice |
| Faust | High | Low | Knows everything, tells you at a price or with a slant |
| Scheherazade | High | Medium | Knows the rules, delivers them as stories you must decode |
| Don Quixote | Zero | High | Confidently wrong about everything. Thinks you need five cards. |
| Virgil | High | High | Most reliable advisor — but only answers what you ask |
| Wife of Bath | Medium | High | Knows some rules, guesses at others, delivers all with equal confidence. Right 60% of the time. |

### Design Implication
Your first draw isn't just picking a companion. It's picking your tutorial voice. The tutorial might be wrong, incomplete, a lie, or a riddle depending on who's talking.

## 11. The Coda Mechanic: Unchosen Actors

### Structure
After every draft, the two unchosen Actors get a brief scene in the margins of the parchment. This is the **coda.**

### Rules
- Short. Beckett-adjacent. Stoppard-adjacent (Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead energy).
- The two actors deal with not being chosen in ways consistent with their personalities
- The scene ends on a punchline, a riposte, or a line worth ending on
- Always interrupted at the last moment by something unusual — a sound, a smell, an object doing something it shouldn't
- In the player onboarding coda specifically: one of the four unclaimed cards does something impossible and vanishes (see Section 12)
- One of the two actors recognizes what happened — something or someone else is still choosing

### Purpose
- Emotional texture between mechanical draft events
- Establishes that unchosen cards have inner lives and opinions
- Seeds the mystery of the Other Chooser
- Creates the player's first choice: which of these two actors to draw
- Different pairings produce different tonal codas (comedic, melancholy, adversarial, tender)

### The Choice
After the coda, the player is presented with the two unchosen Actors and must select one. The framing text captures each actor's response to what just happened:
- *"She knew it wasn't a deal. He knew it was. Choose."*
- *"The guide lost the map. The prince found the question. Choose."*
- *"He always knew. She saw the pattern. Choose."*

The player thinks they're picking a character. They're actually drawing their first card.

## 12. The Other Chooser

### What Happens
During the coda, the four unclaimed cards (Artifacts and Ideas left on the table after the equipment draft) are taken. Not by a player. Not by a team. By something *underneath the parchment.*

### How It Manifests
Each card disappears differently, on-brand:
- The Rosetta Stone might crack and fold into itself like a door closing
- The Voynich Manuscript might grow a tendril and pull itself through the page
- Tabula Rasa might erase itself — a white flash, then nothing, not even a rectangle
- Memento Mori might fall *through* the parchment into a dark hole that seals behind it

### What the Actors Notice
One of the two unchosen actors recognizes what's happening: someone or something is still choosing. After the draft. Outside the draft. Building a hand from underneath the page with the cards nobody wanted.

### The Other Chooser as Rival
- Drew first (an Artifact, out of turn, unauthorized)
- Drew the other unchosen Actor (after the player drew theirs)
- Roughly matched to the player in Act 1 — learning at the same pace
- The player's first adversary: zero-sum duels as the tutorial combat loop
- Must be dispatched before the larger three-way war begins

### The Other Chooser's Hand (from playtest)
The specific unclaimed cards vary per game (generative), but in the playtest they were: a translator (Rosetta Stone), an unreadable text (Voynich), a blank slate (Tabula Rasa), and death (Memento Mori). That's not a junk hand. That's a hand that *decodes, resists, erases, and ends.*

## 13. Player Onboarding

### The Player Is Not the Actor
The Actor is a card in the Player's hand. The Player has their own fiction — a character archetype that emerges through play.

### Player Archetypes (examples, not exhaustive)
- The Collector — climate-controlled archive, first editions, acquisitive
- The Damaged Professor — red-marked manuscripts, tenure pressure, obsessive
- The Frustrated Writer — 3am, cursor blinking, desperate for something real
- Others generated by the LLM based on player input or discovered through play

### Onboarding Sequence

**1. Wake Up.** The player comes to in a setting appropriate to their archetype. The Professor's cluttered office. The Writer's apartment. The Collector's archive. The setting *is* the character.

**2. The Eureka.** They just did something impossible — drew a living idea out of a book. The *how* is specific to who they are:
- The Professor decoded something that shouldn't have been decodable
- The Writer wrote a sentence and the character answered back
- The Collector opened a first edition and something was *inside* it that wasn't printed there
- Each version feels like the culmination of something they'd been circling for years

**3. The Problem.** They did it once and don't know how they did it. The trick needs to be repeatable. The Actor card they drew is alive, opinionated, and can help — but the Actor has its own reasons for helping, its own level of understanding (game savvy), and its own agenda.

**4. The Threshold.** Three cards minimum to play. The Actor tells them this (or doesn't, depending on game savvy and honesty), or they discover it through trying and failing. First quest: get back into a book, figure out the extraction mechanic, draw two more cards.

## 14. Player Motivation: Three Layers

### Layer 1: Curiosity (Surface)
You just pulled a living idea out of a book. Of course you want to do it again.

### Layer 2: Empathy (Emotional Hook)
Your card is fading. The thing you brought into the world is dying because it's out of its book. It talks to you. It has opinions about your apartment. It's getting thinner. You're not just collecting — you're keeping something alive.

### Layer 3: Survival (The Stakes They Don't See Coming)
You drew a card. You're a player now. You didn't sign up for this — or maybe you did, in the eureka moment, without reading the terms. The rule: **lose all your cards, you die.** Not your character dies. Not your game ends. *You.* The first card you drew is also the last wall between you and whatever happens to a player with an empty hand.

### How the Layers Stack
- Early game: exploring (curiosity)
- Mid game: protecting (empathy)
- Late game: surviving (survival)

### Actor Knowledge of the Layers
The Actor may or may not understand or reveal all three layers. Depends on game savvy and honesty:
- Faust archetype: knows about Layer 3 from the start, doesn't tell you until it's useful to him
- Hamlet archetype: doesn't understand the rules well enough to warn you
- Scheherazade archetype: tells you in a story nested inside another story; you won't realize the warning was literal until three sessions in
- Quixote archetype: doesn't understand it's a problem
- Virgil archetype: will tell you if you ask the right question; won't volunteer it

## 15. Game Arc: Three Acts

### Act 1: Tutorial — The Other Chooser
- Player wakes up. Draws first card. Learns the rules (imperfectly, through their Actor).
- The Other Chooser is the mirror rival — roughly matched, learning at the same pace.
- Zero-sum duels as training ground.
- Goal: dispatch the Other Chooser, build initial hand to 3+ cards.
- **Cost:** while you're learning, the two AI factions are getting stronger.

### Act 2: The War You Weren't Invited To
- The Other Chooser is gone. But the Descenders and the Unsilenced (or their generative equivalents) have been building empires.
- Not hands. **Empires.** Cards that grow powerful attract minions of their own (see Section 16).
- Three-way cold war. Direct conflict is expensive — attacking one faction lets the other capitalize.
- Maneuvering, alliances of convenience, proxy wars through minion networks.
- The battle between factions is **bleeding into the player's real world** (see Section 17).

### Act 3: Armageddon of Substance, Idea, and Will
- Three factions with fully leveled decks and entrenched minion networks.
- Reality is halfway dissolved. The parchment and the world are the same thing.
- The Professor's campus is unrecognizable. The Writer's novel is writing itself. The Collector's archive has become a fortress.
- Final confrontation between all remaining factions.

## 16. Minion Networks and Empire Building

### Core Concept
As cards grow more powerful across multiple battles, they may attract **minions** — subordinate entities that operate in the world on the card's behalf.

### Examples (from playtest deck, for calibration)
- Faust might develop a law firm → a lobbying group → a multinational
- Scheherazade might develop a podcast → a 24-hour news network → she *is* the news cycle
- The Wife of Bath might develop a grassroots movement → a political party
- Murasaki might develop an archive → a surveillance apparatus
- Ahab might develop a single ship → a fleet
- Virgil might map something underneath the player's city that nobody knew was there

### Minion Networks as Targetable Infrastructure
- Minion networks can be attacked directly (Military Action) or undermined indirectly (Dirty Tricks, Legal Action)
- Weakening a card's network degrades their power without direct confrontation
- Networks interact with each other — Scheherazade's news network might be running a narrative that strengthens one of your cards while also serving the enemy

### Minion Networks as Obligations
- Every network requires maintenance and defense
- A card's network is an asset *and* a liability
- Losing a network weakens the card and may trigger relationship consequences (animosity toward teammates who failed to defend it)

---

## 17. Reality Bleed: The World as Arena

### Core Concept
The battle between factions doesn't stay on the parchment. It bleeds into the player's world. The player's normal reality becomes the secret battleground where opposing Ideas are being aligned for maximum effect.

### Escalation Curve
- **Early game:** Small synchronicities. A law firm called Foust & Associates. A voicemail that never ends from a woman with a Persian accent. A stranger named Billy Bath talking about his surly wife. Ignorable.
- **Mid game:** Synchronicities you can't ignore. Events start aligning with the thematic content of the factions' chosen Ideas. News stories echo card descriptions. Real-world institutions start behaving like minion networks.
- **Late game:** The synchronicities *are* reality. There's nothing underneath them. The parchment and the world are the same surface. The Descenders and the Unsilenced are fighting across all time in the player's world.

### What's Happening Behind the Scenes
The original draft — the team battle that was about to happen before the player arrived — is now playing out across the player's reality. The Ideas each team chose are being deployed as real-world forces. The Categorical Imperative manifests as a wave of accountability journalism. The Sublime manifests as increasingly extreme weather or architecture or public spectacle. The Social Contract manifests as new legislation that nobody remembers voting for.

### Player Interaction
- Talking to your Actor or paying attention to your environment reveals the bleed
- The player can engage with the bleed (intervene in real-world manifestations) or ignore it (let the factions fight while you build your deck)
- Ignoring it has consequences: the factions get stronger, the bleed gets worse, the world gets weirder
- Engaging has consequences: you're now a visible player in a war you're not yet equipped for

## 18. Action Cards

### Core Concept
A fourth card type. Action cards **do something** to you, to other players, or to the world. They are typed by category but their specific effect is **generated by the LLM at the time of playing**, based on the current game state.

### Four Action Types
| Type | Domain | Speed | Typical Blowback |
|------|--------|-------|-----------------|
| **Legal Action** | Systems, bureaucracy, contracts, rules | Slow but structural, hard to undo | Low variance |
| **Military Action** | Direct force, minion vs. minion, fleet vs. network | Fast but costly, collateral damage | High variance |
| **Dirty Tricks** | Subversion, sabotage, espionage, manipulation | Targets infrastructure, reputation, relationships | Medium variance, can backfire |
| **Plot Twist** | Narrative intervention, reality shifts | Most powerful and most unpredictable | Maximum variance — could be anything |

### How Action Cards Work
1. Player holds a hand of action cards, each with a visible **type** and **point budget**
2. Player chooses *when* to play, *who* to target, and *which card* to perform the action (the performer modifies the card — see below)
3. The LLM generates the **specific effect** based on current game state — relationships, minion networks, faction positions, reality bleed level, narrative history
4. The effect is narrated as a story beat, not a stat change

### Example
Player plays "Legal Action" targeting Faust's law firm network. LLM returns: *"A class action lawsuit surfaces in Delaware. Seventeen former clients of Foust & Associates claim their contracts contained a clause written in a language that does not exist. The Voynich Manuscript is entered into evidence. Faust's network loses two minions and gains an enemy it didn't know it had."*

### Action Cards as Story Engine
Because the LLM generates specific effects at play time, every action card is a narrative event. The same "Dirty Trick" played in different game states produces entirely different stories. The category tells you the domain. The story tells you what happened.

## 19. Blowback Mechanics

### The Point Budget
Every action card has a fixed number of points split between **impact** (what it does to the target) and **blowback** (what it does to you). The split is visible before playing.

Example card:
```
DIRTY TRICK — 7 points
Impact: 5 | Blowback: 2
Blowback probability:
  45% Political
  30% Relational
  15% Territorial
  10% Wild
```

### The Performer Modifies the Card
Which Actor (or minion) *performs* the action shifts the impact/blowback ratio and the probability distribution:

- **Ahab performing Military Action:** Almost all impact, almost no blowback. But the blowback that lands is catastrophic and concentrated.
- **Scheherazade performing Dirty Tricks:** Blowback shifts toward Relational — her tricks are narrative, they unravel alliances, the damage is personal.
- **Faust performing anything:** Probability shifts toward a hidden category — "Call It Due." A growing percentage chance that playing the card triggers a clause in his original bargain. A ticking clock inside your own hand.
- **The Watergate Plumbers (minion) performing Dirty Trick:** 1 impact / 9 blowback, 90% Political. You know exactly what you're getting: almost no effect and near-certain political catastrophe. But maybe that's what you want.

### Blowback Resolution
Three checks, in order:

1. **Severity:** How bad is it? Scaled to how violent/disruptive the action was and the blowback point value.
2. **Direction:** Where does it land? Follows existing fractures. High-animosity relationships in your hand attract blowback. A stable hand absorbs a dirty trick. A fractured hand shatters.
3. **Cascade:** Does the blowback stop or chain? One action can produce four consequences, each logical, none obvious at play time. Bill Gates leaves because of the vampire stunt → tech infrastructure weakens → an Idea you were defending is exposed → an enemy faction contests it → one of your remaining cards develops animosity toward you for letting it fall.

### Second and Third Order Effects
The cascade is often more consequential than the action itself because it operates more diffusely and less predictably. The action you aimed at the enemy may do the most damage to your own hand.

### Engineered Blowback
Advanced play: sometimes you play an action card *to cause your own blowback on purpose.*
- You don't want a card but can't discard it without relationship consequences
- Play an action you know the unwanted card will hate
- The card leaves on its own — defects, walks out, refuses to serve
- You engineered the defection. Took the reputation hit. Freed the slot. Dropped the obligation.
- That's not a misplay. That's Machiavelli.

### Hand as Portfolio
Every card in your hand is an asset *and* a liability. Each comes with obligations — Ideas to defend, networks to maintain, relationships to manage. The real strategic calculus isn't "which cards are strongest" but "which cards are worth the upkeep." Sometimes the answer is: let the blowback happen. Let the card walk. Take the hit. Because the slot it frees and the obligation you drop might be worth more than what it was giving you.

## 20. Playtest Session Log

### Battle 1: Original 3-Round Format (Dracula Hand vs. Zhivago Hand)
- **Format:** 3v3, original system prompt, parchment conceit
- **Hand A:** Dracula, The Principia Mathematica, Zeno's Arrow
- **Hand B:** Dr. Zhivago, November 1972 Mad Magazine, Unknown Dutch Sheet Music
- **Winner:** Hand B
- **Key moments:** Mad Magazine mocking Zeno's Arrow out of existence. The Dutch sheet music resisting Dracula by being too small and too real to consume. The emergence of a new card: "Aria for the End of an Argument."
- **Assessment:** Wild, trippy, compelling — but too much showing off. The novelist voice was getting in the way of the story.

### Battle 2: Revised 5-Phase Format, 1v1 (Ahab vs. Scheherazade)
- **Format:** 1v1 with supporting cards, 5-phase structure
- **Player A:** Captain Ahab + sextant that points to desire + obsession as transcendence
- **Player B:** Scheherazade + unfinished manuscript + stories delay death
- **Arena:** A lighthouse (the idea of a lighthouse). Prize: The Lens of the Lighthouse (focuses meaning inward — shows the point of any story).
- **Winner:** Ahab
- **Key moment:** Ahab recognizing Scheherazade as a colleague — both live in the space between beginning and end. Then walking past her anyway, because she cannot delay a man in love with his own ending.
- **Assessment:** The 5-phase structure is "significantly stronger." Phase 1 makes you care before the fight. Arena as character created emergent effects (the lighthouse's unreliable light as narrative consequence). Leaner, meaner prose.

### Battle 3: Revised 5-Phase Format, 1v1 (Dracula vs. Zhivago — Original Cards)
- **Format:** 1v1 with the original mismatched supporting cards from Battle 1, 5-phase structure, graphic novel narrator constraint
- **Player A:** Dracula + The Principia Mathematica + Zeno's Arrow
- **Player B:** Dr. Zhivago + November 1972 Mad Magazine + Unknown Dutch Sheet Music
- **Arena:** A train station that is also a cathedral that is also a graveyard. A broken clock stuck at 11:59. Prize: A punched train ticket, destination illegible.
- **Winner:** Zhivago
- **Key moment:** The Dutch sheet music restarting the station clock. Time proven real by the simple fact that a piece of music has a duration — it starts, it plays, it ends. Zeno's Arrow shattered by a held whole note. Dracula defeated not by force but by the resumption of time.
- **Card consequences:** Zeno's Arrow destroyed (dormant). Principia recontextualized (now has sheet music in its margins). Dracula diminished (carries a timestamp: 12:00:01). Zhivago unchanged (he continues — that's the point). Mad Magazine leveled up (gold border, cheapness as armor proven). Dutch Sheet Music named: "Stationary Music No. 1 — for the Resumption of Clocks."
- **Assessment:** The graphic novel constraint was "the unlock." Tighter, meaner, hits land harder. The mismatched cards (not chosen to fit the actor) stretched the narrator and produced unexpected connections. Best battle of the session.

## 21. Key Design Principles (Emerging)

1. **The 5-phase structure is the standard.** It gives characters investment before the fight and consequences after.

2. **The arena is a character.** It should tilt outcomes in ways the narrator didn't plan. The stopped clock was just atmosphere until it became the whole battle.

3. **Coherence matters. But so does chaos.** A hand of weak but thematically unified cards can defeat powerful but incoherent ones. But a deliberately incoherent hand can produce the most surprising results.

4. **Cards that don't fit their Actor are more interesting than cards that do.** Ahab + obsession is obvious. Zhivago + Mad Magazine is a stretch that produces emergent narrative. The stretch is where the game lives.

5. **The parchment aesthetic unifies everything.** Cards, narration, UI, the catalog. A markdown file with battle scars IS a parchment written on, erased, stained, and written on again.

6. **Relationship mechanics turn one-off battles into a season.** The draft becomes haunted by accumulated history. Five games in, Hamlet's grudge against Virgil is a specific, personal, narratively rich thing.

7. **Relationship sensitivity varies by character.** The same ledger score means different things on different cards. This makes team composition a social architecture problem, not just a power optimization problem.

8. **Multiple encounter types serve different purposes.** PvP for social drama. Duels for intensity. Raids for cooperation. Grinding for acquisition. The card doesn't care what mode it's in — it just knows its history.

9. **Three card types, three relationship models.** Actors are social (bilateral affinity/animosity). Artifacts are loyal (owner-binding, affinity by default, corruption possible). Ideas are indifferent (one-way affinity from Actors, no reciprocity). This asymmetry is load-bearing — it creates fundamentally different strategic considerations for each card type.

10. **Artifacts become specific at binding.** Before binding, an Artifact is potential. After binding, it's defined — stats generated, owner recorded, provenance begun. The same Artifact in different hands is a different card. This is Tabula Rasa as a game mechanic.

11. **Actors are pawns with motives.** The player moves the pieces. The pieces move themselves. An Actor may take an Artifact for reasons that have nothing to do with the upcoming battle. This creates narrative threads that span sessions and gives characters depth the player has to discover, not dictate.

12. **Every draft is a relationship event.** Choosing someone is an event. Not choosing someone is an event. Equipping an Artifact is a binding with consequences. The pre-battle phase is not setup — it is *play.*

13. **The game is generative, not fixed.** The playtest deck is a tuning fork. The real game generates all cards at session start via LLM. No two games share a card pool. Archetypes recur because they're structurally useful; specific cards are always new.

14. **Your first draw is your tutorial voice.** The Actor's game savvy, honesty, verbosity, and self-interest determine how (and whether) the player learns the rules. The tutorial can be wrong, incomplete, a lie, or a riddle.

15. **Three layers of motivation, each darker than the last.** Curiosity → empathy → survival. The player discovers them in order. The Actor may reveal them out of order, late, or not at all.

16. **The Other Chooser is the mirror.** A rival building from underneath the parchment, roughly matched, learning at the same pace. The training-wheels adversary whose real cost is the time you spend fighting them while the factions grow.

17. **Reality is the resource being consumed.** The faction war bleeds into the player's world. Early game: synchronicities. Mid game: undeniable patterns. Late game: the parchment and the world are the same surface.

18. **Every card is an asset and a liability.** Cards come with obligations — Ideas to defend, networks to maintain, relationships to manage. The strategic question isn't "which cards are strongest" but "which cards are worth the upkeep."

19. **Action cards are typed but not defined until played.** The player holds categories (Legal Action, Military Action, Dirty Tricks, Plot Twist). The LLM generates the specific effect at play time based on game state. The category is the domain. The story is the consequence.

20. **Blowback follows fractures.** Second and third order effects of actions may be more consequential than the action itself. Blowback finds existing cracks in your hand and widens them. Relationship management isn't flavor — it's structural integrity.

21. **Engineered blowback is advanced play.** Sometimes you play an action card to cause damage to your own hand on purpose — provoking a card you want to lose into leaving on its own. The freed slot and dropped obligation may be worth more than the card.
