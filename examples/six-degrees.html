The Chain

Six links. Three books. One circle.

I.
The Wind in the Willows
In Chapter 7, "The Piper at the Gates of Dawn," Rat and Mole travel upriver at dawn and encounter the god Pan on a small island, guarding a lost baby otter. The god of the wild, standing in an English river, playing his pipes.
II.
Pan & Greek Mythology
Pan is a figure from Greek mythology, the half-goat god of shepherds, wild places, and panic. The same tradition gives us the hero Theseus, slayer of the Minotaur, who became Duke of Athens.
III.
A Midsummer Night's Dream
Theseus is literally the first character to speak in Shakespeare's play. "Now, fair Hippolyta, our nuptial hour draws on apace." He is the frame around which the entire dream unfolds.
IV.
Shakespeare & Dumas
Shakespeare was a lifelong obsession of Alexandre Dumas, who adapted Hamlet and other plays for the French stage.
V.
The Count of Monte Cristo
Dumas's great hero, Edmond Dantès, escapes the Château d'If by taking the place of a dead man in a burial sack. He enters the water as a prisoner and surfaces as a ghost.
VI.
The Wind in the Willows — Again
Toad of Toad Hall escapes prison disguised as a washerwoman. It is Dantès by way of English comedy: the same desperate substitution, the same improbable freedom, but gentler and funnier.
↑ The spine — branching upward from Midsummer
•
Pyramus & Thisbe
In Act V, Bottom and the mechanicals perform a catastrophically bad play-within-a-play. The story they mangle — two lovers separated by a wall — is not Shakespeare's invention. He lifted it wholesale.
•
Metamorphoses
Ovid told the story of Pyramus and Thisbe in Book IV, two thousand years ago. Shakespeare read it, loved it, and handed it to the worst actors in Athens.
•
Book 14 — The Sorceress
In Book XIV, Ovid tells of Circe: the goddess who transforms Scylla into a monster, who turns Odysseus's men to pigs. She is Ovid's study in what transformation costs.
•
Circe
Madeline Miller, in 2018, retold it from Circe's perspective. The novel is about metamorphosis — its title is Ovid's title. Two thousand and ten years separate the two books. The thread held.

Five books. Two millennia from Ovid to Miller. Nine degrees.
Every node you add reveals connections that were always there. The web only grows.
