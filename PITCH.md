# World of Guttenberg

## What Is This?

World of Guttenberg is a game where books are alive — not just collectible resources, but living worlds with borders, immune systems, and inhabitants who don't know they're being watched. Players navigate the literary web — the invisible network connecting every book ever written — recruiting characters, forming squads, and waging faction warfare across a map that IS human literature.

The source material is Project Gutenberg — over 70,000 free, public domain books spanning centuries, cultures, and genres. The engine is a modern LLM. The result is a strategy game where the weapon is scholarship and the battlefield is the written word.

We've playtested it. It works. The Dutch sheet music restarted a stopped clock and shattered Zeno's Arrow with a held whole note. Dracula was defeated not by force but by the resumption of time. These aren't scripted moments — they emerged from the collision of ideas on a parchment page.

---

## The World

There is no overworld. There is no hub. There is no fantasy kingdom or space station to return to.

Every world in World of Guttenberg is a book. The Bible is a world. Neuromancer is a world. A 1987 article about touring the Napa Valley is a world. A self-published zine on the free shelf of a comic shop in Cambridge, Massachusetts, is a world.

The map is the literary web — the actual network of influence, reference, adaptation, and theft that connects every text ever written to every other text. Shakespeare connects to Greek mythology through Theseus. Greek mythology connects to Madeline Miller's Circe. Circe connects to Ovid's Metamorphoses. The Count of Monte Cristo connects to Shakespeare through Dumas's lifelong obsession with adapting him for the French stage. Every book is a node. Every influence is an edge. The web is the territory.

The feel is Borges by way of No Man's Sky. A vast, interconnected multiverse of ideas that you traverse, exploit, and fight over — except instead of planets, you're jumping between texts. And instead of a procedurally generated universe, the connections are real. They exist in the actual history of human writing. The LLM knows them. You discover them.

### The Parchment

The interface is a blank page. Everything that happens in the game is written and sketched into existence on that parchment in real time. Battle scenes rough themselves out in hasty ink strokes. Dialogue appears in flowing script and fades as you read it. The margins fill with doodles and marginalia that reflect who you are.

The game looks like the thing it's about.

## The Cards

The game uses three card types in equal proportion — 8 Actors, 8 Artifacts, 8 Ideas — each with a fundamentally different relationship to the player and to each other.

### Actors — Eight faces drawn from ink and argument

Actors are characters. They have personalities, voices, private motivations, and relationships with each other. They remember who they fought beside and who they fought against. They carry grudges. They develop loyalty. They have agendas of their own.

**Scheherazade** — the woman who fights with unfinished sentences. A defensive nightmare. Weak against opponents who don't care how the story ends.

**Captain Ahab** — monomania as siege engine. Unstoppable in a straight line. Useless if the target moves.

**Virgil** — not the poet, the *guide*. The man who walks you through Hell and explains it calmly. His power is that he has already seen the worst thing and continued walking.

**The Wife of Bath** — loud, sexual, theologically dangerous, and absolutely correct about everything she's supposedly wrong about. Cards that depend on dignity weaken in her presence.

Actors track **affinity** and **animosity** toward every other Actor they encounter — independent scores that can coexist. Begrudging respect. Bitter loyalty. Violent passion. The relationships accumulate across battles and haunt every future draft.

### Artifacts — Eight objects that survived their owners

Artifacts bind to owners. Their stats are generated at binding — the same Artifact is different in different hands. They accumulate provenance. A sword that killed a king remembers, even if the new wielder doesn't.

**A Half-Burned Library Card from Alexandria** — what remains of what was lost. Its power is absence. It makes other cards aware of what's missing from them.

**The Voynich Manuscript** — a book that no one can read. Two hundred and forty pages in an alphabet that does not exist. Its power is total resistance to interpretation. It sits on the field and makes every other card doubt its own legibility.

**A Sailor's Journal, Author Unknown, 1787** — water-damaged, mostly illegible. Coordinates for a place that doesn't appear on any map. A door to somewhere. You cannot know where until you play it.

### Ideas — Eight thoughts that outlived their thinkers

Ideas are weather. They don't bond. They don't belong. An Actor can build their whole identity around the Categorical Imperative — and the Categorical Imperative does not know their name.

**The Sublime** — Burke's version. Not beauty — the thing too vast, too terrible to be beautiful, and overwhelms you anyway. The card scales to its opponent. The stronger the enemy, the stronger the Sublime becomes, because it feeds on being overpowered.

**Eternal Recurrence** — Nietzsche's heaviest thought. The card asks one question of every card it touches: *would you choose this again?* Cards that answer yes become unbreakable. Cards that answer no collapse under the weight of their own repetition.

**Memento Mori** — remember you will die. The simplest idea in the catalog. Devastating to cards built on permanence. Irrelevant to cards that already know.

## The Battle

Each battle unfolds in five phases, narrated by the LLM in the voice of a prose-heavy graphic novelist — terse, punchy, thinking in panels.

Each side fields one Actor carrying one Artifact and one Idea.

**Phase 1: The Hands Are Drawn.** How each side selects and relates to their cards. Internal monologue revealing strategy, personality, voice. This is where you start caring about the outcome.

**Phase 2: The Arena.** The arena is a character, not scenery — a place that can tilt outcomes in ways nobody planned. A train station that is also a cathedral that is also a graveyard. A broken clock stuck at 11:59. A lighthouse whose light is unreliable. The arena may contain the prize being fought over.

**Phase 3: The Battle.** The principal action. Can take many forms — physical brawl, outwitting, chase, metaphorical battle spanning years, literal historical battle. Not restricted to real-time combat.

**Phase 4: The Victory and the Loss.** What happened and what it meant.

**Phase 5: The Aftermath.** Card consequences — damaged, destroyed, empowered, transformed — AND world consequences. How does this battle move the larger war?

Stats provide guidance, not determinism. A weak card with perfect thematic resonance defeats a stronger one. Cards that don't fit their Actor produce the most surprising results. The stretch is where the game lives.

## The Literary Web

Every book is secretly about every other book. The threads between them are older than any single author, and they pull tighter the harder you look.

This is the game board.

### Connections

Books connect through real literary history. A direct quotation. A shared character. A structural debt. An acknowledged influence. A stolen plot. These connections are not invented — the LLM validates them against the actual history of literature. You can't fake it. The links either exist or they don't.

The types of connection form a technology tree:

**Tier 1: Direct references.** Book A quotes, adapts, or contains material from Book B. The cheapest traversal. Walk right through.

**Tier 2: Shared authorship.** Same writer, different works. A known corridor. Slightly more resistance — the texts may not want to share.

**Tier 3: Thematic resonance.** Shared motifs, structural parallels, genre conventions, historical influence chains. Requires scholarship to unlock. The player must research and propose the connection; the LLM confirms whether it's real.

**Tier 4: Engineered connections.** You write new text to create a bridge that didn't exist before. Publish a story in your homeworld zine that references a distant node, and you've built a door. The most powerful and expensive traversal type — because you're not navigating the literary web, you're extending it.

Each tier requires different **traversal technology** — unlocked by spending **scholarship**, the game's research currency. Think No Man's Sky: a vast universe of connected nodes, but you need the right jump drive to use each type of connection.

### Immune Systems

Books fight back.

Every text has an immune system proportional to its cultural weight — roughly, how many people have read it, argued about it, referenced it, and care about it continuing to mean what it means. This is the text's readership, its scholarly attention, its place in the collective imagination. All of it pushes back against intrusion.

Bring a Warhammer Space Marine, Tyler Durden, Hamlet, and Hector into Little Women and the book will reject them. The immune system activates. The narrative pushes back. The characters don't fit the tone, the period, the internal logic of the world, and the cumulative weight of millions of readers who know what Little Women is resists the intrusion.

The Bible has billions of antibodies. Shakespeare has centuries of scholarship standing guard. A 1987 magazine article about Napa Valley wine tours? The keys are basically already in the car.

**Resistance scales with the vibe check** — how badly your party clashes with the host text's genre, period, tone, and internal logic. A classical Greek hero entering Ovid faces mild resistance. A cyberpunk hacker entering Ovid faces a wall.

**Failure modes when the immune system triggers:**

- **Hard rejection** — expelled from the text entirely
- **Difficulty scaling** — the narrative turns hostile, everything gets harder
- **Structural damage** — the text itself is degraded by the intrusion
- **Permanent destruction** — the text is destroyed, with cascade consequences

**Weird fiction** — Borges, Danielewski, Lovecraft — tends to have porous immune systems. Strange visitors are on-brand. These texts become safe havens for misfit parties.

**Fan fiction** exists as ephemeral meeting ground — low readership, disposable, a place where characters from different universes can interact with minimal resistance. But popular fan fiction hardens over time, developing its own immune system.

**Literal metaphors** offer a way through. Transform your character into something the host text will accept — a metaphorical translation that makes them compatible. Costs meta-currency. Changes may not fully reverse. The cyberpunk hacker becomes a traveling scribe; the Space Marine becomes a wandering knight. The transformation is the toll.

### Node Warfare

Two things can happen to a node.

It can be **disconnected** — knocked off the network, untraversable, but still intact. No cascade damage. The text still exists in the literary web; you just can't use it as a door. This is the blockade. The siege. Presumably reversible.

Or it can be **destroyed** — gone. And everything downstream that only existed because of that text is now structurally compromised. The cascade fires. Dominoes fall proportional to the destroyed text's influence.

Destroy Hamlet and you'll burn down the game. Half the nodes on the map lose a parent. Routes collapse across factions. The literary web contracts. Mutually assured destruction — not because of a rule, but because the math doesn't work.

Destroy one issue of Cat Fancy from 1993, and it's probably fine.

So disconnection is the scalpel and destruction is the nuke. Most of the war is fought with scalpels, because anybody reckless enough to start destroying canonical texts is going to burn their own house down in the cascade.

The most valuable intelligence in the game isn't military — it's literary. The player who has mapped the most connections, who understands the deepest dependency chains, who knows that destroying *this* obscure 1971 essay collection will surgically cut one enemy route without touching anything else — that player wins. The weapon is scholarship.

### Fast Travel

Fast travel across well-traveled nodes is possible if your party doesn't trigger immune responses along the route. But the more nodes you skip, the higher the probability of a **random encounter** — your party drops into any scene in any text along the way and has to extract themselves without arousing suspicion.

This is the immune system doing spot checks. You're in the express lane, moving through nodes without stopping for the full vibe check. Most of the time you pass through fine. But every skipped node is a dice roll, and the dice are weighted by how much you don't belong.

Your all-female party with the angel-winged member and the heroin addict in a dune buggy — they get flagged in a New England summer romance novel. It's awkward but survivable. Minor episode. Move on.

Same party, same dune buggy, dropped into a le Carré Cold War thriller. Angel wings at Checkpoint Charlie. The text doesn't squint — it activates. The novel's internal logic demands consequences. You don't get an awkward chapter — you get an international incident.

Party composition matters not just at the destination, but across the *entire route*. You're not just asking "can my team survive Neuromancer?" You're asking "can my team survive being randomly dropped into any book between here and Neuromancer?" That's a packing problem. You build your party not just for the mission target but for the worst-case encounter along the way.

## A Mission

Here's how it plays.

Your homeworld is **Nevermind** — a very small, very strange zine, only occasionally self-published, that appears on the free shelf of the Million Year Picnic, a one-room comic shop overflowing into the basement hallway of a parking garage-turned-mall in Cambridge, MA. Readership: maybe dozens. Immune system: essentially zero. Nobody is watching. It's yours.

Your goal: recruit **Benedetto** — a character from The Count of Monte Cristo whose fate Dumas left unresolved after his trial. He's convicted but we never learn what happens to him. The book's immune system has let him go. He's a loose thread the text won't fight you for.

But you can't jump directly from Nevermind to Monte Cristo. You don't control that territory. You don't have the traversal technology for that connection type.

What you do have is a Gifted Author — someone who knows the game, or whose work can serve as an entry point. Madeline Miller. You publish **Nevermind #71**, which contains a short review of Miller's Circe, suggesting there is more to the story than appears and that it may be a bridge to other worlds. That issue — your quest trigger — creates a textual link. A portal disguised as literary criticism.

Now you traverse the chain:

1. **Circe** (Miller, 2018) — Your entry node. Low resistance. Miller is your patron.
2. **Metamorphoses** (Ovid, 8 AD) — Circe's direct source material. Strong immune system. Two thousand years of scholarship watching.
3. **A Midsummer Night's Dream** (Shakespeare, c. 1596) — Connected through Pyramus and Thisbe, the play-within-the-play that Shakespeare lifted wholesale from Ovid. Nuclear-grade immune system. The biggest hub in English literature.
4. **The Count of Monte Cristo** (Dumas, 1844) — Connected through Shakespeare's influence on Dumas. Your destination.

Four immune systems. Four vibe checks. Your starter Actor from Nevermind #1 has to survive passage through Ovid, through Shakespeare, to reach Dumas's Paris — extract Benedetto — and walk back.

Or maybe you find a cheaper route. Some obscure text nobody's thought of that bridges Ovid to Dumas without going through Shakespeare. Low readership. Weak immune system. A back alley through the literary web. The player who finds that shortcut — through scholarship, through actual literary knowledge — has the advantage.

And what if your target isn't Dumas at all? What if you want to snatch a character from Neuromancer? You have to plot a course through the entire multiverse of ideas. Circe to Ovid to... what? How do you get from ancient Greek mythology to 1984 cyberpunk? Maybe through transformation as a theme — Ovid's shapeshifters to Kafka's Metamorphosis to body horror to cyberpunk body modification. Maybe through some obscure 1970s New Wave sci-fi anthology that nobody reads. The routing problem is the game.

## Your First Game

You wake up. You don't know where you are, but the air smells like old paper and something older. You just did something impossible — pulled a living idea from a book. The *how* is specific to who you are. The Professor decoded something that shouldn't have been decodable. The Writer wrote a sentence and the character answered back. The Collector opened a first edition and found something inside it. The Bookhunter was running an extraction and something came out that wasn't in the brief.

The thing you pulled out is an Actor card. It's alive. It has opinions about your apartment. It has a name and a voice and a personality that you didn't choose. It knows things about this world — maybe. Depending on its **game savvy** (how well it understands the rules) and its **honesty** (whether it shares what it knows truthfully), your first card might be a reliable guide, a charismatic liar, or a confidently wrong companion.

It tells you — or you discover on your own — that you need three cards to survive. Your first quest: get back into a book, figure out the extraction ritual, pull out an Artifact and an Idea.

Meanwhile, something is happening in the margins of the parchment. Other cards — the ones you didn't choose — are being taken by something underneath the page. **The Other Chooser.** A mirror rival building a hand from the leftovers. Learning at the same pace you are. Your first adversary.

And while you and the Other Chooser are busy learning, the factions are building empires across the literary web.

## The War

### Act 1: Tutorial

You vs. the Other Chooser. Zero-sum duels as training ground. Build your hand to three cards. Dispatch your rival. Learn that the literary web exists — that books connect, that you can move between them, that other forces are already out there claiming territory. The cost: while you were learning, the factions got stronger.

### Act 2: The War You Weren't Invited To

The Descenders and the Unsilenced have been building not just hands but **territory** — controlling nodes on the literary web, locking down connections, running extraction missions across the map. Faust has a supply chain running through German philosophy. Scheherazade controls the entire Arabian Nights cluster and is expanding into Persian poetry. The Wife of Bath has a grassroots movement colonizing feminist literature.

Three-way cold war on the literary web. Attacking one faction's nodes lets the other capitalize. Disconnecting a hub reroutes everyone's supply lines. Destroying a canonical text cascades unpredictably — you might cripple an enemy and orphan half your own infrastructure in the same move.

And the war is bleeding into your world. Small synchronicities at first — a law firm called Foust & Associates, a voicemail that never ends from a woman with a Persian accent. Then undeniable patterns. Then the synchronicities ARE reality. The parchment and the world are the same surface.

### Act 3: Armageddon

Three factions with fully mapped territories and entrenched positions across the literary web. Reality is halfway dissolved. The cascade math gets existential. The final confrontation between substance, idea, and will — fought across a map that is the sum total of human writing.

## Three Layers, One World

World of Guttenberg operates on three interconnected levels. A single multiverse experienced from three altitudes.

### The Literary Web — Strategy

At the highest level, you're navigating the network of all human writing. Territory isn't land — it's texts. You control nodes, build connections, unlock traversal technology, plan routes, model cascade damage, and wage faction warfare across a map that is real literary history. Scholarship is the currency. The LLM validates connections, simulates opponent moves, and models cascades at graph speed.

### The Narrative — RPG

One level down, you're inside the books. Moving through living texts, navigating immune systems, extracting characters, surviving random encounters during fast travel. Confronting Archons — boss-level pillars of their ideals like Plato, Sherlock Holmes, or Darth Vader — who serve as opponents, patrons, and quest givers simultaneously. The experience is exploration, diplomacy, stealth, and survival within worlds that have opinions about whether you belong.

### The Arena — Card Game

At ground level, you fight. Your deck is built from essences extracted from books. Battles are resolved by the LLM narrating the thematic collision. Every battle tells a story. Every story is unrepeatable.

## Books as Living Resources

### Immune Systems and Cultural Weight

Shakespeare is a fortress — centuries of scholarship, billions of readers, an immune system that will reject anything that doesn't belong. But that obscure 1847 beekeeper's treatise? Walk right in. Obscurity is power.

Books can be entered, navigated, extracted from, damaged, disconnected, or destroyed. The cost of interference scales with readership. The consequences scale with connectivity.

### Unresolved Characters

Every book leaves loose threads — characters whose fates the author never resolved. Benedetto convicted but never sentenced. Eugénie Danglars on the road to Rome, story wide open. Baron Danglars alive but broken with no path forward. These characters are free agents. The text's immune system has released them. They make ideal recruits because nobody is tracking them after the book ends — unless they appear in sequels, adaptations, or other fiction that would tighten the leash.

### Engineered Connections

At the highest level of play, you don't just navigate the literary web — you extend it. Publish a new issue of your homeworld zine with a review, a reference, a short story that creates a textual link to a distant node. You're building wormholes through the multiverse of ideas. The connections are fragile — low readership, easily severed — but they're yours.

### Books as Dungeons

Some books aren't waypoints — they're destinations. Quest inside them. Navigate their immune systems. Extract characters, artifacts, or ideas. Complete the mission and make it back out in one piece.

## Playtested

We built playtest prompts and ran them through Claude. Here's what happened.

**Ahab vs. Scheherazade** — Ahab carrying a sextant that points to desire and the concept of obsession as transcendence. Scheherazade carrying an unfinished manuscript and the idea that stories delay death. Arena: a lighthouse. Ahab won. The key moment: recognizing Scheherazade as a colleague — both live in the space between beginning and end — then walking past her anyway, because she cannot delay a man in love with his own ending.

**Dracula vs. Zhivago** — the absurd original hand. Dracula with the Principia Mathematica and Zeno's Arrow against Dr. Zhivago with a 1972 Mad Magazine and sheet music from an unknown Dutch composer. Arena: a train station that's also a cathedral, a broken clock stuck at 11:59. Zhivago won. The Dutch sheet music restarted the station clock. Time was proven real by the simple fact that a piece of music has a duration — it starts, it plays, it ends. Zeno's Arrow shattered by a held whole note. Dracula defeated not by force but by the resumption of time. A new card was born from the collision: "Stationary Music No. 1 — for the Resumption of Clocks."

**The Ginsfeld Sessions** — a Bookhunter named Dr. Ginsfeld runs a fake therapy group in a basement beneath a library. Four literary characters — Sei Shōnagon, Yvain, Azaro, Fevvers — arrive as strangers. Over twenty beats of dialogue and social pressure, their abilities surface through dice-driven skill checks and passive auras called Filters. Sei tries to read Fevvers and fails — the first time in a thousand years of observation that her assessment has collapsed into paradox. Azaro sees Yvain's absent lion as "a hole in the air shaped like loyalty." Sei advocates for Fevvers over herself. Yvain overrules the decision with battlefield intelligence nobody expected. Azaro senses the mission target through fifty feet of concrete. By the end, four strangers have become a team with genuine interpersonal trust — not because the game told them to, but because their personalities, auras, and abilities collided in ways nobody designed.

These moments weren't designed. They emerged. That's the game.

## Why LLMs Make This Possible

Every concept described above depends on an AI engine that can do three things no fixed system could:

**Validate real literary connections.** The player proposes a route through the literary web. The LLM confirms or denies whether the influence chain is real — real quotation, real adaptation, real historical influence. You can't fake it. The connections either exist in the actual history of literature or they don't. Every game session is partly a research problem.

**Simulate the literary web at graph speed.** Node ownership, connection types, immune system strength, cascade damage, faction movements — these are graph operations. The LLM processes them instantly. The expensive, rich, narrative generation happens only when you actually enter a book. The strategic layer runs cheap because the abstraction is clean.

**Generate unique encounters.** Every extraction is different. Every immune system response is emergent. Every battle is unrepeatable. Every book in the Gutenberg catalog is a genuine, explorable world. The unpredictability isn't a bug — it's the fundamental game mechanic.

Before LLMs, you'd need hundreds of thousands of predetermined interactions. The game would be finite. With an LLM, the entire history of literature is the game board, and every play session discovers connections that were always there.

## The Gutenberg Catalog

Project Gutenberg offers over 70,000 free eBooks. Texts from every era, genre, and culture.

In most card games, you look up the meta online. In World of Guttenberg, the meta is unknowable because every extraction is unique and every route through the literary web is different. Someone finds a weird 1903 anarchist pamphlet and pulls something nobody's ever seen. They post about it and suddenly everyone's racing to find obscure texts — not for the cards inside them, but for the *routes* they unlock. The Gutenberg catalog becomes a treasure map. Players become readers — not because the game forces them to, but because reading is how you find power.

## What We Need

This is an invitation to build something that has never existed.

**Writers and Literary Minds** — Prompt design is game design here. The quality of every extraction, immune system response, and Archon encounter depends on how well we teach the LLM to read and interpret. People who understand literature deeply are as important as people who understand code.

**Game Designers** — The card system needs balancing, the literary web needs economy design, the cascade mechanics need stress-testing. How does scholarship accrue? What's the right cost curve for traversal technology? How do immune systems scale? What's the endgame victory condition?

**Artists** — The parchment interface, the card aesthetic, the literary web visualization, the marginalia. The card catalog already exists in parchment-styled HTML with Wikipedia thumbnail integration. This game's visual language is unlike anything else.

**Prompt Engineers** — The LLM prompts ARE the game engine. Five playtest prompts exist and have been iterated through multiple sessions. Extraction, battle narration, full session, Archon encounters, and mission assembly — each needs continued refinement. New prompts needed for immune system simulation, cascade calculation, and literary web validation.

**Developers** — Python, web, API integration. Building the infrastructure that connects the LLM to game state, the Gutenberg catalog, the literary web graph, and the parchment interface.

The design has been playtested extensively. A 24-card playtest deck exists with full descriptions, team rosters, and relationship mechanics. The battle system has been iterated from a 3-round format to a 5-phase structure that produces significantly stronger narrative output. A mission format has been playtested with full character sheets — ability scores, Conditions, Roll Skills, Filters, Reputations — producing the richest character dynamics of any session. The literary web concept has been prototyped — a working visualization connecting five canonical texts through nine degrees of separation already exists. The game arc is mapped out. The open questions are documented.

What's needed now is people who see this and think: *I want to be part of building that.*

## Cost to Play

The game runs on LLM tokens. Here's what that costs in practice.

A typical session — browse a few books, extract from a couple, fight a few battles — uses roughly 50,000 input tokens and 34,000 output tokens. Using smart model tiering (cheaper models for browsing, better models for battles):

| Play Style | Sessions/Month | Estimated Monthly Cost |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Casual (few times a week) | 12 | $3.60–$6.00 |
| Regular (daily) | 30 | $9.00–$15.00 |
| Heavy (multiple daily) | 60 | $18.00–$30.00 |

A single session costs roughly what a fancy coffee costs — or less. And unlike the coffee, every session is completely unique.

Costs will continue to decrease as LLM pricing drops — which it has been doing aggressively. What costs a dollar today may cost pennies in a year.

## The Name

Guttenberg. The printing press. The original act of democratizing ideas — making books available to everyone, not just the powerful. Five hundred years later, we're taking those democratized ideas and asking: what happens when they're alive, and at war, and you can hold them in your hand?

That's World of Guttenberg.
