# The Gutenberg Engine

In 1822, Charles Babbage built a machine that could think in numbers. In 1440, Johannes Gutenberg built a machine that could speak in pages. One mechanized the mind. The other democratized the word. Neither man lived to see what his machine became.

The Gutenberg Engine is both machines at once — and the thing it computes is story.

---

## What the Engine Is

World of Guttenberg is a game you play alone with an LLM on a parchment page. The Gutenberg Engine is what happens when that page is shared.

The game already has everything it needs to run: a literary web, faction warfare, extraction rituals, immune systems, cascade mathematics, cards that remember their own history. What it doesn't have is persistence beyond a single player's session. Your Scheherazade fights brilliantly and nobody sees it. Your route through Ovid to Dumas is elegant and nobody learns from it. Your discovery that a 1903 anarchist pamphlet connects to Neuromancer through three intermediate nodes — that dies when you close the window.

The Engine is the infrastructure that catches all of it.

A traditional tabletop RPG requires a human game master who remembers everything — every NPC's grudge, every quest thread left dangling, every consequence deferred. The game master is the most expensive component in any RPG. They burn out. They forget. They cancel on Thursdays.

An LLM doesn't cancel on Thursdays.

The Engine is a DM-less campaign platform. The LLM maintains persistent world state across sessions — your Actor's Conditions carry forward, your Reputations spread, your faction's territorial gains hold, the nodes you disconnected stay dark. You leave and come back and the literary web remembers what you did to it. Your cards are markdown files that accumulate history like scar tissue. The parchment doesn't reset. It accretes.

Players drop in and out. A session can last twenty minutes or four hours. The Engine holds the world between sessions the way a bookshelf holds a novel between readings — patiently, completely, without forgetting your place.

The game becomes something you live in rather than something you boot up.

## The Two Machines

Babbage's Difference Engine was designed to compute polynomial functions — to mechanize the work of human calculators who sat in rows and ground through tables of logarithms by hand. The machine replaced the drudgery. The human still had to ask the question. But once the question was asked, the machine could answer it faster, more reliably, and without getting tired. Babbage never finished building it. Ada Lovelace saw further — she saw that the engine could manipulate symbols, not just numbers. That it could compose music. That the machine's real purpose was not calculation but *pattern*.

Gutenberg's press replaced the scribe. Before 1440, every book was a manuscript — handwritten, unique, expensive, controlled. The press made text reproducible. Cheap. Uncontrollable. The first thing Gutenberg printed was the Bible, and within fifty years the press had destabilized every institution that depended on controlling who could read. The machine's real purpose was not printing but *access*.

The Gutenberg Engine combines both operations. It mechanizes the game master — the most labor-intensive role in collaborative storytelling — using an LLM that can hold world state, narrate encounters, validate literary connections, and simulate faction warfare without tiring, forgetting, or canceling. And it democratizes the game world — making persistent, narrative-rich campaign play available to anyone with a browser and a subscription, not just people lucky enough to have a dedicated group and a tireless DM.

Babbage's engine became the computer. Gutenberg's press became the internet. What does the Gutenberg Engine become?

We don't know yet. But we know it computes in stories and publishes in worlds, and that nobody has built the thing it is going to be.

## The Hub

The pitch document opens with a declaration: *There is no overworld. There is no hub.*

The Engine changes that — but not in the way you'd expect.

The hub is not a place inside the game. There is still no fantasy tavern where your characters gather between quests. The parchment remains the game interface — intimate, singular, yours. The Engine is the table the parchment sits on. The room the table is in. The building that holds the room.

It is where sessions are recorded and browsable. Every mission you've run, every battle your Actors fought, every extraction ritual and its consequences — archived as scrollable narrative on parchment-styled pages. Your Scheherazade's full biography, written not by you but by the accumulation of everything she's done across dozens of sessions. Character timelines that read like novels because they are novels — emergent ones, authored by the collision of game mechanics and literary history.

It is where the literary web is visible. The six-degrees visualization that already exists as a prototype — five canonical texts connected through nine degrees of separation — scaled to the full scope of play. Your explored territory glowing. Faction boundaries marked. Disconnected nodes dark. Contested corridors pulsing. A map you can zoom into, trace routes across, and plan campaigns on. The strategic layer made spatial.

It is where your card catalog lives. Every Actor, Artifact, and Idea you've ever extracted, displayed on parchment with their full history — who they've fought, who they've bonded with, what Conditions they carry, what Reputations follow them. A library of characters that other players can browse, admire, and covet.

The game generates literature about literature. The Engine is where that literature is kept.

## The Cartographers

A game built on 70,000 texts and the infinite connections between them cannot be mapped by one team. It shouldn't be. The literary web is too vast, too deep, too weird. The obscure 1847 beekeeper's treatise that secretly connects to Virgil's Georgics — no design team is going to find that. A retired entomologist in Devonshire who happens to play the game is going to find that.

The Engine opens the door to three kinds of creator.

### Plot Architects

Mission design in World of Guttenberg is already modular — a target text, an objective, complications, required traversal, opposition. The Engine lets anyone build missions and share them.

A literature professor in São Paulo designs a four-node extraction run through Brazilian modernism — Machado de Assis to Clarice Lispector through Oswald de Andrade's Manifesto Antropófago, ending at a node in Portuguese colonial correspondence that nobody has explored. She specifies the immune system intensities, the likely vibe check failures, the organizational entanglements. She uploads it. A player in Edinburgh runs it that evening with his own team and his own cards, and the LLM narrates an entirely different story through the same architecture.

The mission is the skeleton. The LLM provides the flesh. Every run is unique, but the bones hold.

### Web Cartographers

The literary web has edges nobody has discovered yet. The Engine rewards the people who find them.

A retired high school teacher in rural Ontario reads a 1912 pamphlet on prairie agricultural cooperatives and realizes it quotes, verbatim, a passage from Kropotkin's *Mutual Aid*. That's a Tier 1 connection — direct reference — between two nodes nobody thought to link. He proposes it. The LLM validates it against the source texts. The connection is real. A new edge appears on the shared literary web. His name is on it. Every player who uses that route benefits from a path he built.

The first person to validate a connection between a Meiji-era Japanese railway manual and Walt Whitman's *Leaves of Grass* through their shared obsession with the democratic sublime of infrastructure — that person has built a road nobody knew existed. That road persists. Other players traverse it. The cartographer's name stays in the marginalia.

### Artists

The parchment aesthetic is distinctive — hasty ink strokes, flowing script, marginalia that reflects who you are. The Engine gives visual artists a place to contribute to that aesthetic and be credited for it.

Card portraits. Text illustrations. Literary web visualizations. Parchment marginalia styles that other players can adopt. Arena sketches. Faction insignia. The visual language of the game is rich enough to sustain a community of artists, and the Engine is where their work lives — attributed, browsable, and woven into the play experience.

## The Reading Room

Most games produce fans who talk about the game. World of Guttenberg produces fans who talk about books.

This is structural, not incidental. The game's core mechanic — traversing the literary web — rewards literary knowledge. The player who has actually read Ovid spots the connection to Dumas faster than the player who hasn't. The player who stumbled through a used bookstore in Portland and read half of a 1960s beat poetry collection might be sitting on a route nobody else can see. Reading is strategic advantage. It always has been. The Engine makes it social.

The Reading Room is where players discuss the actual texts from Project Gutenberg — not as homework, not as fandom, but as intelligence. Someone reads an 1880s travelogue through the Balkans and finds a reference to a Serbian folk epic that connects to the Homeric tradition through a shared structure of catalog poetry — lists of warriors, lists of ships, lists of the dead. That's a three-node shortcut from an undefended text to a heavily fortified one. She posts about it. Now six people are reading Serbian folk epics, not because anyone assigned them but because the strategic implications are irresistible.

Book clubs form around regions of the literary web. A group of players running campaigns through Russian literature starts a reading circle for Dostoevsky — not to appreciate him (though they do) but because understanding the internal logic of his texts makes immune system navigation easier. Someone defending a cluster of Romantic poetry starts annotating Shelley because knowing which poems reference which gives you traversal data.

The Reading Room is where the Bookhunter shares intelligence and the Damaged Professor finds peers. Where the Frustrated Writer discovers that her deep knowledge of an obscure genre is, for the first time in her life, a tactical asset. Where the Collector learns that the 1927 first edition he's been hoarding connects to a text nobody else can reach.

Most games consume content. This one generates readers. The Engine is where those readers find each other.

## Connected Worlds

Every player's literary web is a subgraph — a personal territory carved from the larger map of all human writing. Your explored nodes, your controlled routes, your faction boundaries, your disconnected corridors. The shape of your war.

The Engine lets you connect that shape to someone else's.

When two players link their worlds, their subgraphs overlap. The edges between shared nodes become live. Characters can cross. Consequences can propagate. A cascade event in one world ripples into the other if they share nodes — and since both players are navigating the same literary web, they almost certainly share nodes.

Here is what that looks like.

You've spent weeks building a power base in nineteenth-century French literature — Dumas, Hugo, Balzac, Flaubert. Your faction controls the corridor from Monte Cristo to Les Misérables. Your Actor Benedetto has accumulated three pages of relationship history and carries two Conditions from a near-fatal extraction in Madame Bovary.

Another player has built outward from American transcendentalism — Thoreau, Emerson, Whitman, Melville. Her Ahab has been through six campaigns and hates everyone on his team except a quiet Artifact that reminds him of the sea.

You connect your worlds. The edge between Melville and the broader network of nineteenth-century maritime fiction activates. Suddenly her Ahab can enter your territory through the naval chapters of Les Misérables. Your Benedetto can reach Moby-Dick through the French translations that Melville read.

And the consequences are live. If she destroys a node in her territory that your routes depend on — if she nukes a text that sits between two of your critical waypoints — the cascade hits you. Your roads collapse. Your faction loses access. Her local war becomes your supply chain crisis.

Characters who cross between connected worlds arrive as complete individuals — their full markdown files, their relationship histories, their Conditions, their Reputations. They are not stat blocks. They are people who have been somewhere and done things and remember all of it. When Ahab walks into your game carrying six campaigns of accumulated damage and resentment, he doesn't arrive as a clean copy. He arrives as himself.

This is where the game becomes geopolitical. Alliances form not between characters but between worlds. A guild of players who control overlapping regions of the literary web can coordinate faction warfare across a territory none of them could hold alone. An attack on one world is an attack on the network. The cascade math scales.

And somewhere in the connected web, a node that nobody thought was important turns out to be the only bridge between two allied territories. The player who mapped it — some quiet cartographer who validated an obscure connection six months ago — is suddenly the most strategically significant person in the network.

## What Grows Here

The player archetypes in the core game — Collector, Damaged Professor, Frustrated Writer, Bookhunter — are not just onboarding paths. They are community identities.

In the Engine, these identities find each other. The Collectors form guilds organized around literary periods and obsess over completionism — every Actor extractable from the Victorian novel, every Artifact hidden in the footnotes of Enlightenment philosophy. The Damaged Professors build the Reading Room into something that looks, from a certain angle, like the graduate seminar they always wanted and never got. The Frustrated Writers gravitate toward Tier 4 engineered connections — writing the zines, the reviews, the short fiction that extends the literary web — and discover that their writing now has readers, because it has strategic utility. The Bookhunters run extraction services for other players, scouting routes, testing immune systems, bringing back intelligence from deep in uncharted texts.

These communities aren't designed. They emerge, the way communities always emerge — from shared purpose, shared territory, and shared obsession. The Engine provides the infrastructure. The literary web provides the territory. The players provide everything else.

What makes these communities unusual is their substrate. Most gaming communities form around a game. Most book communities form around books. The Engine sits at the intersection — a place where gamers become readers and readers become gamers and the difference between the two stops mattering. A community of people who care about the same 70,000 texts, for reasons that are simultaneously strategic and genuine.

That community does not exist anywhere else. The Engine is where it lives.

## The Cost of Running an Engine

The base game runs on LLM tokens — roughly the cost of a fancy coffee per session. The Engine adds infrastructure: persistent world state, cross-session memory, community features, the shared literary web, connected world coordination.

The good news is that persistence is cheaper than regeneration. A session that can load previous state from stored markdown files spends fewer tokens rebuilding context than a session that starts from scratch. Community-contributed content — missions, validated connections, artist work — amortizes across every player who uses it. The expensive part of the game (rich narrative generation) stays local to individual sessions. The Engine layer (state management, web visualization, community features) is conventional infrastructure, not LLM inference.

| Tier | What You Get | Estimated Monthly Cost |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| Solo | The base game. One parchment. No persistence between sessions. | LLM tokens only ($4–$15) |
| Engine | Persistent world state. Session archive. Literary web map. Reading Room access. | $8–$20 on top of tokens |
| Connected | Everything above plus world-linking, cross-world play, guild infrastructure. | $12–$25 on top of tokens |

LLM costs have been falling aggressively and will continue to. Infrastructure costs scale with users, which means they also scale with community contributions — more players means more validated connections, more missions, more shared content, and lower per-player costs for the community layer.

The Engine is a subscription to a living literary world. The question isn't whether it's worth the price. The question is what it's worth to have a game that makes you read, a community that cares about books, and a world that remembers everything you've done in it.

## What the Engine Becomes

Gutenberg's press became the pamphlet, the newspaper, the novel, the textbook, the zine. Every form of mass communication for five centuries traces back to movable type.

Babbage's engine became the calculator, the mainframe, the personal computer, the phone in your pocket. Every computation you've ever run traces back to a Victorian machine that never quite got finished.

The Gutenberg Engine starts as a platform for a card game about books. It will not stay that. A system that makes literature navigable, playable, and collectively maintained by a community of people who are simultaneously gaming and reading — that system is something new. Not a game platform. Not a social network. Not a book club. Not a library. A thing that combines all four and is none of them. A living map of human writing, maintained by the people who play inside it.

We don't have a word for that yet.

One machine computed. The other published. This one does both — and the raw material is every book ever written.

That's the Gutenberg Engine.
