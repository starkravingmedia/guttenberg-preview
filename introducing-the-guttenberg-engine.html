What Is This?

A game where books are alive

The Guttenberg Engine is a game where books are alive — not just collectible resources, but living worlds with borders, immune systems, and inhabitants who don't know they're being watched. Players navigate the literary web — the invisible network connecting every book ever written — recruiting characters, forming squads, and waging faction warfare across a map that IS human literature.

The source material is Project Gutenberg — over 70,000 free, public domain books spanning centuries, cultures, and genres. The engine is a modern LLM. The result is a strategy game where the weapon is scholarship and the battlefield is the written word.

You choose your squad from the vast, teeming universe of human literature — characters who carry voices, grudges, agendas, and histories older than most nations. Then you explore that universe together. But be careful: the literary web is not a museum. It is a living ecology, and every expedition erodes it. Every character you pull from a text leaves a wound in the narrative. Every mission frays the fabric a little more. The parchment doesn't reset. It scars.

What follows are two completely machine-generated playtest sessions — preambles to the action, where two teams competing for the same objective are recruited by Bookhunters with very different aims and methods.

The Mission

What both sessions are about

Shared Mission Brief
Target: A young soldier named Andrei — a minor figure from a sprawling war epic — has realized he is fictional and is attempting to cross into a pastoral romance two shelves over. He believes that if he can enter the love story, he won't have to die in Chapter 41. He's wrong — the pastoral romance is a closed text, and forcing entry will collapse both narratives.
The Breach: A character crossing between texts creates a momentary structural vulnerability — a seam in the Literary Web's fabric. The two operators pursuing this target want very different things from that seam.
Complication: The war epic is emotionally resonant to one of the team members. It will recognize their affinity and try to keep them, offering them a role in its narrative, a place that feels like belonging. The text isn't malicious. It's lonely. It's losing a character and it wants a replacement.
Information Asymmetry: Ginsfeld has the map but doesn't know who's coming for him. Kade knows who's coming for her but doesn't have the map. This is the mission's hidden geometry.

Two Operators

Same mission. Opposite methods.

Dr. Donald Ginsfeld, PhD
Bookhunter — Player Character
Antiquities dealer. Bookhunter. Runs a "support group" for unmoored literary characters beneath the Metro Library every third Wednesday. The coffee is real. The therapy is fake. The recruitment is the point.
Objective: Stabilize. Save Andrei. Prevent collapse.
Lisle Kade
Bookhunter — Opposition Operator
Faster than you. Better funded than you. Unencumbered by the dead weight of ethics. She runs a YouTube show as a recruitment filter because it's cheap, it's scalable, and if the recruits don't come back, there are always more where they came from. She has never appeared on camera.
Objective: Weaponize the breach. Extract rare textual material. Profit.

What the Engine Computes

These two sessions emerged from the same system — the same characters, the same mechanics, the same literary web stretching across seventy thousand books. But the stories they generated are entirely different, because the operator changed, the method changed, and the characters responded accordingly. One session is a quiet basement therapy circle. The other is a chaotic internet spectacle. Both are recruitment drives for the same mission. The contrast is the point.

What makes this compelling is the emergence. Nobody wrote these stories. They arose — from the collision of characters who carry persistent memory, operators with incompatible philosophies, and a literary universe that reacts to being explored. A character who appears in one session carries the consequences into the next. Reputations shift. Alliances form across narrative boundaries. The stories generated by The Guttenberg Engine are not authored — they are computed, and they surprise even the people running the game.

This is what makes the engine fascinating to engage with: every session is unrepeatable. The same squad entering the same text on different nights will produce different outcomes, because the characters have changed since last time, the text has scarred from the last expedition, and the operator has learned something they can't unlearn. The parchment accumulates. The web erodes. The stories get stranger and more personal the deeper you go.

The raw material is every book ever written. The engine computes what happens when you send the characters back in.
