Actors

Eight faces drawn from ink and argument

I.
…
Hamlet Sidelined
The prince who cannot act. Every decision is a soliloquy. His power is paralysis weaponized — nothing moves while Hamlet is thinking, and Hamlet is always thinking.
II.
…
Scheherazade Battle-Tested Unsilenced
The woman who fights with unfinished sentences. Defensive nightmare. Weak against opponents who don't care how the story ends.
III.
…
Captain Ahab Battle-Tested Descenders
Monomania as siege engine. Unstoppable in a straight line. Useless if the target moves.
IV.
…
Lady Murasaki Shikibu Unsilenced · Capt
Author of the world's first novel, playing a character in her own diary. She observes. That's it. But her observations change what she's looking at. The original unreliable narrator, except she's reliable — everyone else is performing.
V.
…
Don Quixote Sidelined
The man who rewrites reality by refusing to read it correctly. Windmills become giants because he says so. His delusion is a creative act. Dangerous because he's wrong about everything and it doesn't matter.
VI.
…
Faust Descenders
The scholar who traded knowing for having. His card smells like a deal you already signed. Every alliance with Faust costs something. Every victory is a payment coming due.
VII.
…
Virgil Descenders · Capt
Not the poet. The guide. The man who walks you through Hell and explains it calmly. His power is that he has already seen the worst thing and continued walking. Nothing shocks him. Nothing stops him. He just narrates the descent.
VIII.
…
The Wife of Bath Unsilenced
Loud, sexual, theologically dangerous, and absolutely correct about everything she's supposedly wrong about. She breaks decorum the way a brick breaks a window. Cards that depend on dignity or authority weaken in her presence.

Things

Eight objects that survived their owners

IX.
…
A First Folio (1623)
Not Shakespeare. The book. The object that decided which plays survived and which didn't. It is an act of editorial violence disguised as preservation. It chooses what lives. It has opinions.
X.
…
The Rosetta Stone
The key that unlocks but does not explain. It translates between worlds without understanding either one. Powerful as a bridge. Dangerous because bridges go both ways.
XI.
…
A Half-Burned Library Card from Alexandria
What remains of what was lost. The card is mostly ash. But the catalog number is still legible, and the book it points to is one that no longer exists. Its power is absence. It makes other cards aware of what's missing from them.
XII.
…
Gutenberg's Original Press
The god-machine. The thing that broke the monopoly on knowledge and started every war that followed. It doesn't create ideas. It copies them. Rapidly. Indiscriminately. It turns one into a thousand. This is either salvation or plague depending on what you feed it.
XIII.
No image.
The card
doesn't want
to be found.
A Sailor's Journal, Author Unknown, 1787
Water-damaged. Most pages illegible. What survives: coordinates for a place that doesn't appear on any map, a sketch of a coastline that matches nothing, and a single entry — "We arrived. God help us." The card is a door to somewhere. You cannot know where until you play it.
XIV.
…
The Communist Manifesto (First Edition, 1848)
Forty-eight pages that broke the world in half. The card vibrates at a frequency that agitates every other card on the field. It doesn't fight — it organizes. Weak cards near it become a collective. Strong cards near it become a target.
XV.
…
A Child's Primer, Boston, 1761
"In Adam's fall / we sinned all." The ABCs as theology. The first thing you learn to read is that you're guilty. The card is small, simple, and cuts to the bone. It teaches. What it teaches is damage.
XVI.
…
The Voynich Manuscript
A book that no one can read. Two hundred and forty pages of text in an alphabet that does not exist, illustrated with plants that do not grow. It has been studied for a century and yielded nothing. Its power is total resistance to interpretation. You cannot decode it. You cannot dismiss it. It sits on the field and makes every other card doubt its own legibility.

Ideas

Eight thoughts that outlived their thinkers

XVII.
…
The Categorical Imperative
Kant's razor. Act only according to the maxim you could will as universal law. The card tests everything it touches for hypocrisy. Powerful against pretense. Useless against anything that never claimed to be moral in the first place.
XVIII.
…
Negative Capability
Keats's term for the ability to remain in uncertainty without grasping after fact and reason. The card does nothing. That is its power. It sits in doubt comfortably while other cards panic. It cannot be pinned down because it has already accepted that being pinned down is not the point.
XIX.
…
The Sublime
Burke's version. Not beauty — the thing that is too vast, too terrible, too powerful to be beautiful, and overwhelms you anyway. Storms. Mountains. God's silence. The card scales to its opponent — the stronger the enemy, the stronger the Sublime becomes, because it feeds on being overpowered. Weak against small, mundane things that don't trigger awe.
XX.
…
Memento Mori
Remember you will die. The simplest idea in the catalog. A skull on a desk. The card does one thing: it reminds every other card on the field that it is temporary. This is devastating to cards built on permanence. Irrelevant to cards that already know.
XXI.
…
The Social Contract
Rousseau's wager. We gave up freedom for safety and now we have neither. The card binds. It creates rules between cards that didn't agree to them. It is the foundation of civilization and the first act of tyranny, simultaneously, and it cannot be played without changing the relationship between every card on the field.
XXII.
…
Tabula Rasa
Locke's blank slate. The mind begins empty. Everything you are is written on you by experience. The card erases. Not violently — gently, like a tide smoothing sand. It strips other cards of their accumulated history, their learned behaviors, their inherited trauma. What remains is potential. Whether potential is a gift or a void depends on what fills it next.
XXIII.
…
The Allegory of the Cave
Plato's prisoners, watching shadows on a wall and calling them real. The card does not attack — it reveals. It turns the light on and shows every card on the field that what they thought was true was a projection. Devastating in theory. In practice, most cards prefer the shadows. The ones that don't are never the same afterward.
XXIV.
…
Eternal Recurrence
Nietzsche's heaviest thought. What if you had to live this life — every moment, every pain, every joy — again and again, forever? The card asks a single question of every card it touches: Would you choose this again? Cards that answer yes become unbreakable. Cards that answer no collapse under the weight of their own repetition.
